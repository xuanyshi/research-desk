Causal Evidence Extraction and Triangulation in Crisis Reports using Large Language Models: A ReliefWeb-based Study

Yuanjun Zhang, Mourad Oussalah

Findings of the Association for Computational Linguistics: ACL 2026, 32478–32491

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Large language modelsEvidence triangulationCausal evidence extractionInformation extractionEvidence provenanceModel distillationHumanitarian NLP

30-second read

Query conditioning and snippet grounding yield weighted F1 90.73% for the best proprietary pipeline and 94.15% after Llama-3.1-8B distillation. LoE=0.865 measures cross-context directional concentration without testing cross-design bias independence.

Question
How can auditable intervention–outcome causal statements be extracted from long crisis reports, and do their directions converge across disaster and source contexts?
Design
An engineering method and case study using about 8,000 English ReliefWeb reports from 2000–2024, a 100-report expert benchmark for two-stage LLM extraction, and disaster×source cells for cash-assistance and food outcomes.
Evidence strength Moderate
What was assessed
Four label-layer F1 measures on 100 reports with 220 expert relations, plus directional distributions for about 19,600 extracted records across 19 disaster×source cells.
Downgrade triggers
The test set is small and has no reported annotator agreement; fixed configurations lack repeated runs or error bars, and LoE depends on strength weights, polarity labels, smoothing, and cell definitions.
Impact on interpretation
The results support auditable extraction and cross-context agreement summaries. LoE cannot be read directly as cross-design causal strength or intervention-effect magnitude.

Full deep read

The paper adds query conditioning, evidence snippets, and context stratification to crisis-report extraction. Extraction is strong; its LoE score measures directional concentration across contexts rather than cross-design causal strength.

The authors

Yuanjun Zhang lists the University of Oulu and LUT University as affiliations, while corresponding author Mourad Oussalah is at the University of Oulu. The work was supported by the EU Horizon 2020 and CHANSE Digital Emergency Communication project, DIGeMERGE; the group has also published on explainable multimodal disaster-severity assessment.

The problem

ReliefWeb reports are long and multi-topic. Recall-oriented keyword search retrieves documents in which cash assistance may appear only in passing, and an unconstrained LLM can turn peripheral text into intervention–outcome relations. The paper aims to produce structured records containing an intervention, outcome, direction, strength, and supporting passage, then summarize directional agreement across disaster and source contexts.

The approach

The corpus contains English full-text ReliefWeb reports from 2000–2024 retrieved with “cash assistance.” The authors retain four disaster types—flood, drought, epidemic, and earthquake—and five source groups: academic, government, media, NGO, and Red Cross/Red Crescent. This leaves 8,029 reports. Domain experts annotate 220 relations in a random test sample of 100 reports.

Extraction has two stages. Stage 1 computes intervention–outcome candidates within the query-specified intervention class and attaches supporting snippets; the query condition is intended to suppress retrieval-induced over-extraction. Stage 2 classifies each candidate as increase, decrease, or no clear change and assigns weak, moderate, or strong evidence using the snippet and full report. Intervention and outcome matches use BERTScore≥0.8. Strength is scored only when direction is correct, with 0.5 credit for adjacent-category errors.

Before triangulation, polarity normalization maps positively and negatively framed outcomes such as food security and food insecurity onto a common improvement direction. Each record enters a disaster×source cell. Weak, moderate, and strong receive weights 0, 1, and 2; directional counts receive Laplace smoothing with α=0.1. Non-empty cells are averaged equally. The largest of the three global directional probabilities is linearly rescaled from 1/3–1 into LoE 0–1. Temporal trajectories repeat the calculation cumulatively by year.

The best proprietary pipeline also generates training labels for two Llama-3.1-8B LoRA adapters, one per stage. The 100 expert-annotated reports are excluded from distillation.

Key results

Two-step Qwen-Plus with query conditioning and snippet grounding reaches weighted F1 90.73%. Llama-3.1-8B rises from 75.12% without fine-tuning to 94.15% after LoRA SFT. The weighted score combines intervention, outcome, direction, and strength at 0.30, 0.30, 0.20, and 0.20.

The selected proprietary pipeline extracts 19,568 records from 8,029 reports. The food-outcome analysis retains 19 non-empty disaster×source cells and reports PP=0.91 and LoE=0.865. The cumulative series peaks in 2017 at PP=0.953 and LoE=0.929 before softening modestly from 2018–2024. Polarity labels cover 941 outcome strings occurring at least three times, representing 13,554 records or 69.27% of all extractions.

How solid is the evidence

The extraction study includes an expert test set, ablations, and explicit scoring rules. Query conditioning improves results consistently across three API models. The test set contains only 100 reports, however, and the paper gives no inter-annotator agreement. A BERTScore threshold may count loosely similar relations as matches. The ACL responsible-research checklist says fixed configurations were run once, without means, standard deviations, or error bars. Teacher-generated distillation labels can pass systematic model errors to the student.

For LoE, the computational object is the largest cell-averaged directional probability. Dominance by one direction triggers a larger score; the practical effect is a 0–1 measure of concentration. The calculation does not align causal estimands or test whether biases are independent across reports, disasters, or sources. Giving weak evidence a weight of zero removes it from directional counts. Equal cell weighting limits domination by high-volume sources but leaves shared reporting bias untouched.

A sentence in a report claiming that cash assistance improved food security is a causal statement in text, not necessarily a controlled effect estimate. English-only accessible text, one retrieval phrase, and restricted disaster and source categories narrow generalizability. The authors appropriately frame the output as decision support requiring human review and state that strength labels describe reported evidence, not effect magnitude or population impact.
