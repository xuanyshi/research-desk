How to use directed acyclic graphs: guide for clinical researchers

Timothy Feeney, Fernando Pires Hartwig, Neil M Davies

BMJ 388 (2025):e078226

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Causal DAGClinical researchEstimandExpert consensusSensitivity analysisReporting

30-second read

DAGs should precede data and results, and omitted nodes or arrows are strong assumptions. The workflow improves transparency, but a consensus graph can still be wrong and does not quantify bias.

Question
How can clinical observational studies carry a DAG through estimands, data collection, covariate selection, sensitivity analysis, and reporting?
Design
A seven-step applied guide using heavy alcohol use and five-year mortality, moving from population and question through expert consensus and data choice to primary and quantitative bias analysis.
Evidence strength Methods guide
What was assessed
Target population, exposure, outcome, consensus DAG, minimal sufficient sets, and unavailable or mismeasured variables.
Downgrade triggers
A missing key variable or serious measurement error triggers new data, additional collection, or sensitivity analysis; multivariable regression does not erase an omitted node.
Impact on interpretation
The steps can become a clinical-study checklist. Correct execution does not establish that the causal structure is true.

The seven-step clinical DAG loop

Full deep read

This seven-step guide connects estimands, consensus DAGs, data choice, statistical analysis, and bias sensitivity analysis into one clinical-research workflow.

The authors

Timothy Feeney is affiliated with the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill; Fernando Pires Hartwig with the Federal University of Pelotas and the University of Bristol; Neil M Davies with University College London, Bristol, and the Norwegian University of Science and Technology. The authors work in epidemiology and causal-inference methods.

The problem

DAG use in clinical research is increasing, yet a review of 234 articles found incomplete reporting of assumptions and adjustment sets. This article seeks to turn DAGs from publication graphics into traceable design tools that can change data choice, analysis, and interpretation.

The approach

Using heavy alcohol use and five-year mortality, the authors propose seven steps: define population, exposure, outcome, and estimand; identify variables through literature and experts; iterate to consensus and preregister; choose data and measurement from the graph; choose analysis; design sensitivity analyses for missing or mismeasured variables; and publish the DAG, code, and deviations.

The object for adjustment is the set of backdoor paths in the consensus graph. A variable set is triggered when it blocks all confounding paths without blocking the target causal path; the practical effect is specification of primary covariates. Missing key variables trigger new data, a different dataset, or quantitative bias analysis rather than an automatic unbiased estimate.

Key results

This is a methods guide, with no new clinical effect estimate. The alcohol example yields two minimal sufficient sets: socioeconomic position, care access, and heavy alcohol use; or care access, heavy alcohol use, and family history of alcohol abuse. Adding potentially unmeasured family support can eliminate any sufficient set composed only of measured variables.

Steps 4–6 connect the graph to data: identify required measurements, disclose gaps, and translate unmeasured confounding and measurement error into sensitivity-analysis parameters. Step 7 calls for publication of the graph and reproducible code.

How solid is the evidence

The recommendations rest on DAG theory, applied reviews, and author experience. They are appropriate as reporting guidance, but no comparison shows that the seven-step workflow reduces bias. Expert-consensus thresholds and edge-level evidence grades are unspecified. A carefully executed workflow can still produce a wrong consensus graph.

The article emphasizes that omitted nodes and arrows are strong assumptions. DAGs do not quantify bias magnitude, infer direction from data alone, or automatically solve changing adherence, time-varying confounding, and inconsistent intervention definitions.
