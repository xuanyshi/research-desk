Development and application of an evidence-based directed acyclic graph to evaluate the associations between metal mixtures and cardiometabolic outcomes

Emily Riseberg, Rachel D. Melamed, Katherine A. James, Tanya L. Alderete et al.

Epidemiologic Methods

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Evidence-based DAGDAG constructionConfounder adjustmentMetal mixtures

30-second read

Only 43% of 74 testable conditional-independence statements are supported, and 608 permuted graphs perform better. The workflow is auditable, but this structural test does not show superiority over random permutation.

Question
Can a metal-mixture DAG be built from review evidence and structurally tested against conditional independencies in a real cohort?
Design
An evidence-based DAG and cohort pilot, using 42 reviews to graph five metals and three cardiometabolic outcomes, then comparing San Luis Valley Diabetes Study support with 1,000 node-permuted graphs.
Evidence strength Limited
What was assessed
An arc-source ledger from 42 reviews, 1,795 cohort participants, 74 testable independencies, 1,000 permuted graphs, and pilot metal–outcome models.
Downgrade triggers
Some metal-to-outcome arcs are inserted because they are analysis targets. One paper is enough to add an arc, evidence quality is not graded, and environmental and occupational exposure nodes are missing.
Impact on interpretation
The study offers a transparent literature-to-DAG-to-adjustment workflow and a structural test, but the data do not establish that the proposed DAG is better than plausible alternatives.

Reading adviceStart with Figure 1 and the four construction steps, then go directly to the permutation comparison in Figure 2. Treat application results as pilot associations, not validation of every edge.

Evidence-based DAG construction and test

04

Full deep read

The literal precedent for the name "evidence-based DAG": one PubMed search turned 42 reviews into a causal graph, then used to pick confounder adjustment sets.

The authors

Senior author Laura Corlin is an environmental epidemiologist at Tufts University who applies causal-inference methods to observational studies of environmental toxicants; first author Emily Riseberg (Tufts) worked in her group on urinary metals and cardiometabolic outcomes.

The problem

Environmental exposures can't be randomized — assigning people to a toxic metal is unethical — so you're left with observational data, which needs confounding adjustment. A DAG is the standard tool for drawing the causal structure and reading an adjustment set off it.

But environmental epidemiology has almost no "evidence-based" DAGs: building one takes a literature review, and no one had empirically tested whether a literature-built DAG actually fits data.

For metal mixtures and cardiometabolic outcomes, none had ever been published. The authors fill two gaps at once: a method to build a DAG from the literature and test it, and the specific metal-mixture DAG itself.

The approach

Five metals: arsenic, cadmium, manganese, uranium, tungsten. Three outcomes: cardiovascular disease, fasting glucose, systolic blood pressure. One design decision comes first — force an arc from every metal to every outcome, because those associations are the ones they intend to test.

The graph's evidence comes from a single PubMed search: MeSH terms for the five metals AND the three outcomes AND (review / systematic review / meta-analysis), restricted to 2011–2021, English, human studies, plus five articles added from the authors' prior knowledge — 42 in total (As n=22, Cd n=17 the most common).

From each they extracted authors, year, journal, population, exposure, exposure sources, outcome, and main results, then wired the graph in DAGitty.

The rest of the nodes were added in four steps: identify exposure sources from the literature (diet, air, smoking, soil, drinking water); identify outcome risk factors from consensus statements (sex, age, obesity, education, ethnicity, income, physical activity, alcohol); for cross-outcome risk-factor combinations, run a two-word PubMed search ("smoking diabetes"); then the same two-word search for risk-factor–risk-factor and source–risk-factor links.

The bar for an arc borrowed two criteria from ESC-DAG (Ferguson 2020): biologically plausible and temporally accurate, with direction set by timing and background knowledge (education precedes adult diet, so education → diet).

The key move is a very permissive inclusion rule: a single peer-reviewed article suggesting an association is enough to draw an arc, because omitting a real arc biases you more than adding a spurious one. Every arc's source is logged in Appendix A, and a second reviewer confirmed each arc.

The genuinely new step is testing the graph. Map the nodes onto the San Luis Valley Diabetes Study (n=1,795), delete the unmeasured environmental-exposure node, have DAGitty emit every implied conditional-independence statement, test each by regression, and count p>0.05 as support.

Then run the same test on 1,000 DAGs with the same skeleton but randomly permuted node labels as a null. The application side uses the single-door criterion (Pearl 2009) to pull a minimal sufficient adjustment set, fit with BKMR, linear mixed effects, and Cox.

Key results

The DAG implies 81 conditional-independence statements, 74 testable, of which 43% are supported by SLVDS (sample sizes 1,265–1,795). The null gives the most striking number: of 1,000 permuted DAGs, 608 had more than 43% supported — so this "evidence-based" DAG beat only about four in ten random graphs and did not outperform chance.

The minimal sufficient adjustment set is identical across all three outcomes: environmental exposure, smoking, diet.

The application findings are secondary: As roughly linear with fasting glucose, Mn U-shaped (under the larger adjustment set); Cd positive with systolic BP under the minimal set but flattened or reversed once environmental-exposure proxies are added (p-trend 0.07), read as confounding by environmental exposure; no metal associated with CVD; no within-mixture interaction.

The abstract lands on one line: evidence for As and Mn with fasting glucose.

How solid is the evidence

The "evidence-based" label is thinner than it sounds. The metal→outcome arcs aren't derived from evidence at all; they're forced in to be tested, and the paper never separates evidence-arcs from design-arcs. The inclusion rule is crude: one article equals one arc, with no weighting by design or quality, and conflicting reviews get an arc anyway (As–CVD is genuinely inconsistent at low doses).

Direction is a judgment call — the authors admit income and alcohol could run either way. Only secondary literature, 42 papers, a single engine, not a systematic review. It's reproducible in that the query and Appendix A are printed, but the node and arc decisions rest on manual plausibility plus a second reviewer: auditable, not mechanized.

The validation is the best idea in the paper, and it failed — the graph lost to roughly 60% of random permutations. The authors' defenses all hold (permuted DAGs may be biologically absurd, e.g. an arc into age; p-only with no effect sizes; the missing environmental-exposure node), but they amount to saying the test as run can't actually certify the DAG.

So we get a construction procedure and a validation idea, neither shown to work on this dataset.
