Diabetes and Cancer: A Consensus Report

Edward Giovannucci, David M. Harlan, Michael C. Archer, Richard M. Bergenstal et al.

Diabetes Care 33(7):1674–1685, 2010 (joint ADA/ACS consensus report)
CancerDiabetes

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ PharmacoepidemiologyConfounding by indicationHyperinsulinemiaMetformin

30-second read

Diabetes is associated with higher risks of liver, pancreatic, endometrial, colorectal, breast, and bladder cancer and lower prostate-cancer risk. Treatment effects remained causally unresolved because of confounding by indication, reverse causation, and short follow-up.

Question
Which diabetes–cancer associations are credible, and what was known about shared risks, mechanisms, and glucose-lowering therapies?
Design
An ADA–ACS consensus report synthesizing epidemiologic, clinical, mechanistic, and drug-safety evidence by cancer site and medication class.
Evidence strength Moderate
What was assessed
Site-specific relative risks, shared risk factors, insulin and IGF mechanisms, and evidence on metformin, TZDs, sulfonylureas, incretin therapies, and insulin.
Downgrade triggers
This is a 2010 consensus, when pharmacoepidemiology relied heavily on short observational follow-up and had not fully incorporated later immortal-time-bias critiques or completed large trials.
Impact on interpretation
The report is a useful domain map and bias checklist for a diabetes–cancer DAG, but drug conclusions require updated target-trial, randomized, and site-specific evidence.

Reading adviceRead site-specific associations first, then shared risks and mechanisms, and use Table 1 to check medication conclusions. Do not pool all cancers or interpret observational drug associations as treatment effects.

03

Diabetes–cancer evidence framework

04

Full deep read

Diabetes really is linked to several cancers, but the mechanism and the causal role of glucose-lowering drugs were still unsettled in 2010 — this ADA/ACS consensus mapped the battlefield and honestly fenced off every unknown.

The authors

The writing group was co-chaired by Edward Giovannucci, a nutrition/epidemiology researcher at the Harvard School of Public Health, and David Harlan, a diabetes physician at UMass.

Members included Michael Pollak (McGill, a long-time researcher on insulin/IGF and cancer), Susan Gapstur (American Cancer Society), and Laurel Habel (Kaiser Permanente Division of Research).

The report was written independently by the group after a consensus conference convened jointly by the American Diabetes Association (ADA) and the American Cancer Society (ACS) in December 2009.

The problem

Diabetes and cancer are both common and rising, and they co-occur in the same person more often than chance would predict. In 2009 a batch of analyses suggesting the long-acting insulin analog glargine might raise cancer risk caused an uproar, which is why the ADA and ACS sat down together to turn a scattered literature into one panorama.

The report splits into four questions: is there a meaningful association between diabetes and cancer incidence/prognosis, what risk factors do the two diseases share, what are the possible biologic links, and do diabetes treatments change cancer risk or prognosis. What was missing wasn't data points but a framework that could weigh association, mechanism, confounding, and drug signals together.

The approach

This is not an empirical study but a structured synthesis of the existing literature — the method is the consensus review itself. What has value is its analytical frame.

For every association the authors force themselves to separate three explanations: diabetes causes cancer directly (e.g., hyperglycemia itself), diabetes is merely a marker of underlying biology (insulin resistance, hyperinsulinemia), or the two are dragged together by shared risk factors (obesity, inactivity, diet) — the third being confounding.

Mechanistically they bet on the insulin/IGF axis: hyperinsulinemia drives proliferation and anti-apoptosis through the insulin receptor and IGF-I, insulin also lowers hepatic IGFBP-1/2 to raise free IGF-I, and it lowers SHBG to raise the bioavailability of sex steroids.

Hyperglycemia (the Warburg effect) is explicitly downgraded — in type 1 diabetes animal models tumors actually grow more slowly, so hyperglycemia alone doesn't drive growth.

The drug section is where the craft shows: metformin, TZDs, sulfonylureas, incretins, insulin/analogs are each walked through, and each flags the traps of observational studies — most sharply confounding by indication (metformin tends to go to patients with short diabetes duration and no liver/kidney disease) and comparator choice (is the sulfonylurea "increase" the drug causing cancer, or the metformin in the comparator arm protecting?).

Key results

The direction and magnitude of the associations are the most concrete part. Relative to non-diabetics, diabetes raises liver, pancreatic, and endometrial cancer to about twofold or higher, colorectal, breast, and bladder to 1.2–1.5-fold, prostate cancer is lower, and lung is essentially unrelated.

Reverse causality for pancreatic cancer (abnormal glucose may be a consequence of the tumor) is handled by restricting to diabetes preceding diagnosis by at least 5 years, and the association survives; the lower prostate risk holds both before and after PSA screening spread, so it isn't detection bias. For prognosis, breast cancer with diabetes carries a 5-year mortality HR of about 1.39.

On risk factors, the Diabetes Prevention Program's intensive lifestyle arm cut diabetes incidence by 58%, most of it from weight loss; bariatric surgery drops cancer risk in obese women to RR 0.58–0.62 (mostly breast and endometrial), with no association in men.

Drug conclusions are restrained: metformin tracks with lower cancer risk in observational studies; TZDs are inconsistent (a rosiglitazone trial meta-analysis shows no significant change); sulfonylureas show some elevation relative to metformin; glargine has higher IGF-1R affinity and mitogenic potency than human insulin, but a 5-year glargine-vs-NPH RCT saw no excess (57 vs 62 cases), while the larger ORIGIN trial was underway but powered for cardiovascular endpoints.

How solid is the evidence

This is a narrative consensus; its strength rides on the underlying studies, not on new data it generates. Incidence associations for common cancers are fairly stable (consistent across cohorts), but the causal reading is left wide open — direct carcinogen, marker, or shared confounding, the authors don't settle it. The softest part is the drugs.

They honestly list the traps: confounding by indication, comparator choice, too few site-specific cancer events for adequate power, follow-up too short for newer drugs.

But the era limited their tools: this 2010 paper predates Suissa's 2012 systematic critique of immortal time bias in metformin studies — only afterward did the field realize the "metformin is anti-cancer" observational signal was substantially inflated by immortal time and other time-related biases. So its optimism about metformin should be discounted today.

Its repeated insistence on not pooling all cancers (a null lung association dilutes real signals) does hold up.
