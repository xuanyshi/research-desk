Comparative effects of drugs for adults with overweight or obesity: systematic review and network meta-analysis

Kailei Nong, Qingyang Shi, Xinran Xie, Yang Wang et al.

BMJ 394:e372161, 2026 (BMJ Rapid Recommendations)
Obesity

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Network meta-analysisSystematic reviewEvidence gradingRandomized controlled trialsComparative effectivenessGLP-1Obesity drugsClinical guideline

30-second read

Tirzepatide and CagriSema reduce weight by about 15% at one year, while several effective drugs increase gastrointestinal events and discontinuation. Hard-outcome benefits are concentrated in subcutaneous semaglutide, and no quality-of-life improvement reaches the 10-point MID.

Question
How do 19 obesity drugs trade off weight loss, cardiovascular outcomes, quality of life, and adverse effects?
Design
A systematic review and network meta-analysis of 262 RCTs and about 99,800 adults with overweight or obesity, comparing 19 drugs with lifestyle modification across 24 GRADE-rated outcomes.
Evidence strength Higher
What was assessed
A 262-trial treatment network, weight and body composition, mortality and cardiovascular outcomes, quality of life, discontinuation and adverse events, and outcome-specific GRADE ratings.
Downgrade triggers
Indirect comparisons across eras and drugs rely on transitivity. Many hard outcomes and novel agents have low certainty, median follow-up is 26 weeks, and cardiovascular benefit comes mainly from high-risk trials.
Impact on interpretation
The analysis supports one-year benefit–harm decisions and guideline development, but network rankings should not be treated as the long-term best drug for every population.

Full deep read

Pooling 262 obesity-drug trials into one network, tirzepatide and CagriSema deliver the most weight loss at a year (~15%), but bigger losses come with more harms and discontinuation, most drugs don't improve quality of life, and only subcutaneous semaglutide shows cardiovascular and mortality benefit.

The authors

Led by Sheyu Li (Department of Endocrinology and the Chinese Evidence-based Medicine Center, West China Hospital, Sichuan University), with Kailei Nong among the co–first authors, and a heavyweight methods roster: GRADE co-founder Gordon Guyatt (McMaster), Per Olav Vandvik and Thomas Agoritsas of the MAGIC Evidence Ecosystem Foundation, diabetes epidemiologist Kamlesh Khunti, and obesity specialist Carel Le Roux.

It's part of the BMJ Rapid Recommendations (a BMJ–MAGIC collaboration), with results meant to feed a global guideline panel, following the same living-network-meta-analysis model they used for type 2 diabetes drugs. Published in BMJ.

The problem

Obesity drugs have proliferated — GLP-1s, GIP/GLP-1 dual agonists, triple agonists, plus older agents like phentermine-topiramate and naltrexone-bupropion.

Clinicians, payers, and patients must choose among a dozen-plus drugs, but prior network meta-analyses mostly covered only approved drugs and only weight loss, without putting the newer agents, the overall benefit-harm balance, and the certainty of evidence transparently side by side.

What was missing was a landscape covering all available obesity drugs across patient-important outcomes beyond weight (cardiovascular, mortality, quality of life, body composition, adverse events), each tagged with GRADE certainty, to support genuinely informed decisions.

The approach

A systematic review and network meta-analysis, PRISMA-NMA reported and PROSPERO registered, done as a BMJ Rapid Recommendation. They searched Medline, Embase, and Cochrane through November 2025, including RCTs of ≥12 weeks comparing an obesity drug against lifestyle modification, placebo, or another drug, in adults with overweight or obesity (with or without cardiovascular/metabolic complications).

The value of a network meta-analysis is that it compares many interventions at once, including pairs never tested head-to-head (via indirect comparison), provided the network's transitivity/consistency holds. Synthesis used frequentist random-effects models for the main results plus Bayesian dose-response models, with heterogeneity and inconsistency checked.

Risk of bias used Cochrane RoB 2, and certainty was GRADE-rated for each of 24 outcomes.

Subgroups by dose, duration, and key patient characteristics, plus sensitivity analyses, were done. The point of the whole exercise isn't a ranking but a base for a guideline panel to move from evidence to risk-stratified recommendations.

Key results

262 trials, 99,791 participants, 19 drugs, follow-up 12–172 weeks. Weight loss at one year vs lifestyle alone (moderate-to-high certainty): tirzepatide -14.9% (95% CI -16.0% to -13.9%), CagriSema -14.8%, oral semaglutide -10.9%, orforglipron -9.9%, subcutaneous semaglutide -9.8%, phentermine-topiramate -8.1%; investigational agents (retatrutide, mazdutide) may reach 13–14.6% but at very low certainty.

The cost is symmetric: discontinuation due to adverse events was highest with orforglipron, naltrexone-bupropion, liraglutide, phentermine-topiramate, CagriSema, and oral semaglutide (RR 1.9–4.2); gastrointestinal events and fatigue rose too, with naltrexone-bupropion's fatigue RR at 8.9 (331 more per 1,000 over a year). On body composition, tirzepatide cut fat mass the most (25.7%) but also lean mass the most (8.3%).

On hard outcomes, only subcutaneous semaglutide was tied to lower all-cause mortality (RR 0.81, 0.72–0.93) and myocardial infarction (0.72), largely driven by cardiovascular outcome trials in high-risk groups; subcutaneous semaglutide and tirzepatide lowered heart failure risk.

No drug convincingly reduced kidney failure or improved quality of life beyond the minimally important difference (43 trials, 45,663 participants; all improvements <5 points, MID of 10).

How solid is the evidence

This is the most rigorous tier of this literature: an RCT network, item-by-item RoB2, GRADE on each of 24 outcomes, a separate dose-response model, and an explicit plan for a guideline panel to move from evidence to recommendation.

The thing to watch is the network meta-analysis's Achilles heel, transitivity: ranking drugs never compared head-to-head assumes trial populations, comparators, and outcome definitions are comparable enough; if the newer-drug trials differ systematically in population or placebo response from the older ones, indirect comparisons drift — not a paranoid worry for obesity drugs, where early-agent and GLP-1-era trial populations differ.

Second, certainty varies by outcome: weight loss is moderate-to-high, but hard outcomes and quality of life are often low certainty with short follow-up, so "no benefit" is often "not measurable." Third, the mortality/MI benefit rests almost entirely on a few high-risk CVOTs and should be discounted when extrapolating to general weight-management populations.

GRADE-tagging these certainty differences one by one is exactly what makes it more credible than a bare ranking.
