30-second read

The report provides an organization-level governance standard for DAGs, shared language, evidence metadata, and versioning. Most causal edges still originate in expert judgment under sparse evidence and do not replace effect identification or statistical validation.

Question
How does NASA turn roughly 30 astronaut health and performance risks into a shared, evidence-annotated, and updateable DAG system?
Design
An HSRB technical guide documenting the pilot launched in 2020 and specifying node and edge admission, four evidence levels, harmonized terminology, countermeasures, time and feedback, community review, and configuration management.
Evidence strength Normative guidance
What was assessed
Approximately 30 Human System Risks, admission rules for nodes and edges, four LOE categories, three countermeasure types, and worked Acoustics and SANS diagrams.
Downgrade triggers
Risk teams judge LOE from publications and subject-matter expertise. Speculative status requires only temporality and analogy; the report gives no inter-rater, predictive, or decision-impact evaluation.
Impact on interpretation
The framework can support auditable domain-DAG governance. The truth, magnitude, and intervention consequences of individual edges still require study-design and data-based validation.

Full deep read

The operating manual for how NASA's Human System Risk Board hand-builds, reviews, and version-controls its ~30 risk causal DAGs.

The authors

Authored by NASA's Human System Risk Board team led by Erik L. Antonsen, who chairs the board and trained in both aerospace engineering (PhD) and medicine (MD). This NASA technical memorandum sets standardized guidance for risk teams building and updating DAGs.

The problem

NASA tracks about 30 astronaut health and performance risks, all deriving from five unchangeable spaceflight Hazards (altered gravity, radiation, isolation and confinement, hostile closed environment, distance from Earth).

These risks were historically studied in silos, which missed the real situation: injury to the human system in spaceflight is multifactorial and cumulative, stacking from launch onward and sometimes across a crew member's post-career life.

The Board used to score each risk on its own with a Likelihood×Consequence rating and a red/yellow/green light — scoring that can't see the synergies between risks, where a minor issue harmless on its own gets amplified in another body system or at a later time.

In January 2020 the HSRB began a pilot to use DAGs to turn "how risk flows causally" into a shared mental model across all stakeholders. This TM is the guidance for how risk-custodian teams build and update those graphs.

The approach

The core isn't an algorithm; it's a set of conventions plus governance. Every risk DAG starts at one or more Hazards and ends at one or more Mission Level Outcomes, with contributing factors in between; each arrow is defined as a falsifiable causal hypothesis that can be added, removed, or redrawn as evidence changes.

Graphs come in two tiers: Narrative DAGs for communication, reviewed by the Board and configuration-managed; Detailed DAGs, finer-grained and meant for network analysis, which are too specialized to go before the Board.

To compose the individual graphs into one network, terminology must be harmonized and neutral — for "altered gravity" alone, 10 groups had used 6 different names, so names are forced to a common list and continuous variables are preferred. Feedback loops are banned; where genuinely needed, they are unrolled with time-indexed nodes. First drafts are drawn in Dagitty.

The whole thing runs on crowdsourcing plus configuration management: custodian teams draft, the HSRB (10 standing members, 100–200 attendees per meeting) runs a formal review and comment-resolution, and disagreements are settled by the Board or its Chair against evidence standards.

Key results

The deliverable is a set of reusable components.

The node vocabulary is pre-defined: 5 Hazards and 8 Mission Level Outcomes fix the two ends; graph theory's exogenous/endogenous nodes are renamed External/Integral Factors; countermeasures split into monitoring, prevention, and intervention, each with hard drawing rules — a prevention points at the node it is meant to stop, an intervention is not drawn back to the treated condition but to its downstream node.

There are also category nodes with nesting, grey Risk nodes drawn with incoming links only, decision nodes, and time nodes like Effective Exposure Duration. An edge carries two properties: a Level of Evidence (LOE) and a strength of influence (path coefficients or joint-probability tables where data exist).

LOE comes from six Bradford Hill criteria on a four-tier scale with inverted numbering — 4 Speculative, 3 Weak, 2 Moderate, 1 Strong; Speculative = Temporality + Analogy is the threshold for inclusion; each source first passes a quality gate, and poor methodology caps at Weak.

Dotted lines mean weak/speculative, solid lines moderate/strong. A node qualifies if it meets Speculative and has at least one link, or on documented SME concern. New terms must be filed with the office, discussed at the Board, and signed off by the Chair; by January 13, 2022, initial DAGs for all 30 risks had passed.

How solid is the evidence

Its value is process auditability: configuration management plus a comment-resolution trail is a traceable organizational memory of why the graph looks the way it does, and every edge is written as a falsifiable hypothesis. But the rigor rests almost entirely on people.

LOE is assigned by hand, the quality gate is a per-paper human judgment, and Bradford Hill is a qualitative heuristic with only four levels and no automated grading; "SME concern" can justify inclusion on its own — honest, but also the hardest thing to audit.

The strength-of-influence layer is mostly vaporware: the text repeatedly says path coefficients go in "if sufficient data are available," while in practice most edges carry only an LOE and no weights, so the "Bayesian network" is potential, not present.

The authors own two weaknesses: once a DAG is baselined it can anchor the community, and how much detail to show "remains more art than science." Authority sits with the Board and Chair — reproducible inside NASA, but not a portable algorithm. And with 30 graphs taking hundreds of people two years, it does not scale.
