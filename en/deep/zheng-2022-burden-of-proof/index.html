The Burden of Proof studies: assessing the evidence of risk

Peng Zheng, Aleksandr Y. Aravkin, Christopher J. L. Murray

Nature MedicineCAS 1 TopJCR Q1IF 52.5

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Evidence gradingRisk assessmentMeta-analysisDose-response

30-second read

The BPRF combines magnitude and uncertainty into a conservative bound; only 22 of 180 relationships receive four or five stars. The grade reflects conservative association strength, not causal identification or GRADE certainty.

Question
How can effect size, heterogeneity, and uncertainty for a risk–outcome relationship be compressed into a reproducible conservative evidence grade?
Design
A methods and large-scale meta-analysis using constrained-spline MR-BRT to estimate mean risk functions and null-nearest BPRFs, then assigning ROS values and one-to-five-star grades to 180 GBD risk–outcome pairs.
Evidence strength Moderate
What was assessed
Dose–response evidence for 180 pairs, constrained splines, study-level bias covariates, between-study heterogeneity, BPRF, ROS, and star grades.
Downgrade triggers
Shared residual confounding cannot be removed reliably when it dominates the input literature. Publication bias is flagged but not corrected, and animal evidence is excluded.
Impact on interpretation
ROS is useful as an updateable association-strength annotation, but should not be used directly as causal-edge confidence or a clinical recommendation grade.

Reading adviceRead Table 1 for grade thresholds, then compare the strong and weak examples in Figures 1–4. Keep the mean risk curve, BPRF, and causal effect conceptually separate.

03

BPRF evidence-scoring pipeline

04

Full deep read

For each "risk factor → outcome" relationship, fit a conservative lower-bound risk function (BPRF), turn it into an ROS, and compress that into a 1–5 star rating — a reproducible, algorithmic grade of evidence strength. A machine version of GRADE.

The authors

The team is at the Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation (IHME), University of Washington. Lead author Peng Zheng specializes in statistical modeling and meta-analysis software; senior author Christopher Murray directs IHME and leads the Global Burden of Disease study.

The problem

How strong a risk factor–outcome relationship is gets graded today (GRADE, Cochrane) by expert judgment — poorly reproducible and not comparable across pairs; in the authors' words GRADE is "very helpful, [but] cannot be implemented algorithmically." Conventional dose–response meta-analysis usually imposes log-linearity for tractability and, tellingly, computes between-study heterogeneity but doesn't fold it into the overall uncertainty.

Risk factors with modest effects get waved away with "residual confounding." The authors want a data-driven, reproducible, cross-pair-comparable measure of evidence strength to feed GBD's Comparative Risk Assessment — pitched as a complement to GRADE/Cochrane, not a replacement.

The approach

The core object is the Burden of Proof Risk Function (BPRF): among all risk functions consistent with the data, take the one closest to "no relationship" (RR = 1) — the 5th-percentile curve for harmful risks, the 95th for protective.

The intuition is "even read most conservatively, the effect is at least this big," and it reflects both effect magnitude and uncertainty. The model is MR-BRT (mrtool): a Bayesian, trimmed, mixed-effects spline meta-regression.

Steps: (1) PRISMA extraction, mapping outcomes to GBD causes, imputing unreported exposure intervals.

(2) Estimate the shape with B-splines, no log-linearity assumption, modeling each observation as a ratio of integrals of the spline over the alternative vs reference exposure intervals — which handles inconsistent reference groups and exposure ranges; monotonicity and a max-slope (biological plausibility) constraint enter as linear inequalities on the spline derivatives; a 50-model spline ensemble with randomized knots removes knot sensitivity.

(3) Code six GRADE-style bias domains (population representativeness, exposure, outcome, reverse causation, confounding control, selection bias) as effect modifiers on the signal slope, rank them with Lasso, and add them one at a time under Gaussian priors.

(4) Capture between-study heterogeneity γ with a study-specific random slope; γ is underestimated when studies are few (can hit 0), so take its 95th quantile via the Fisher Information Matrix, and combine that with the fixed-effect posterior for the final UI. (5) Detect publication bias with Egger's regression — flag only, no correction.

(6) Average the log BPRF over the 15th–85th exposure percentile, signed, to get the risk-outcome score (ROS): higher = stronger, ROS < 0 = the conservative reading can't reject the null.

ROS is binned into 1–5 stars.

Key results

Star thresholds (verbatim): ROS < 0 is one star, 0–0.14 two, 0.14–0.41 three, 0.41–0.62 four, > 0.62 five — and those cutpoints are just ln 1.15, ln 1.50, ln 1.85, so harmful and protective are symmetric.

Four worked pairs: smoking→lung cancer, 5 stars (RR 5.11 at 20 pack-years, average risk increase 106.7%, ROS 0.73); SBP→ischemic heart disease, 5 stars (RR 2.38 at 140 vs 100 mmHg, ROS 0.70); vegetable intake→IHD, protective but only 2 stars (RR 0.81 at 100 g/day, average 12.1% reduction, ROS 0.13); unprocessed red meat→IHD, a nominal 2 stars with a BPRF averaging 0.01, essentially on the null (ROS 0.01, only +1.04% risk).

Across GBD's 180 pairs, only 22 (12.2%) earned 4–5 stars while 112 (62.2%) got 1–2. The exposure window is stable: ROS correlates 0.98 when swapped to 10–90 or 5–95.

How solid is the evidence

The key caveat: ROS collapses "evidence strength" and "effect size" onto one axis — red meat gets 2 stars not because the studies are few or weak, but because the conservative lower bound sits at the null.

A five-star rating is not high-quality evidence in the GRADE sense. It means that the conservative bound remains large.

Observational confounding is not resolved. It enters as a coarse study-level bias covariate, and the authors concede that covariate adjustment cannot repair a literature in which most input studies share bias. The analysis stays in the association and dose–response domain, without do-calculus, adjustment-set identification, or a potential-outcomes analysis.

Third, the trimming level (keep the 90% most self-coherent points) is a user-set heuristic; publication bias is detected but not corrected; animal evidence is excluded entirely. The authors list eight limitations — commendably candid.
