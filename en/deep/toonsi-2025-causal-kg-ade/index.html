Causal knowledge graph analysis identifies adverse drug effects

Sumyyah Toonsi, Paul N. Schofield, Robert Hoehndorf

Bioinformatics

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Causal inferenceKnowledge graphMediation analysisAdverse drug effectsCovariate selection

30-second read

Graph structure automates hypothesis generation and covariate selection. The best method reaches 0.907 precision on literature-supported causal edges and finds about 30,700 positive ACMEs among comorbidity candidates, but relies on untestable sequential ignorability.

Question
Can a causal knowledge graph generate adverse-drug-effect hypotheses at scale and select confounders for mediation analysis?
Design
A methods and cohort application constructing a drug–disease CKG and testing drug-mediated disease progression in UK Biobank and MIMIC-IV.
Evidence strength Moderate
What was assessed
Disease progression, indications, side effects, and ICD-10 hierarchy in the DD-CKG, plus ACME, precision, recall, and external similarity validation in two observational cohorts.
Downgrade triggers
Disease severity, medication timing, and dose are poorly measured. High precision is concentrated in literature-supported edges, and overlap with expert gold standards is small.
Impact on interpretation
The study supports knowledge-driven hypothesis generation and covariate selection as an engineering workflow, but novel candidates are not confirmed adverse reactions.

Adverse-effect discovery pipeline

Full deep read

Mark causal edges on a knowledge graph and it auto-generates "drug mediates two diseases" hypotheses and confounder sets; run mediation analysis on UKB and MIMIC-IV and it both reproduces known adverse reactions and surfaces new candidates.

The authors

From the Bio-Ontology Research Group (BORG) at KAUST, led by corresponding author Robert Hoehndorf, whose group works on biomedical ontologies, knowledge graphs, and machine learning over structured biomedical knowledge. First author Sumyyah Toonsi is also at KAUST; co-author Paul N. Schofield is at the University of Cambridge.

The problem

Rare adverse drug reactions that surface only after a drug ships are often missed in trials because they're infrequent or delayed, so post-marketing surveillance has to catch them.

That task needs two things at once: background knowledge (disease and drug taxonomies, disease progression, indications, known side effects) and co-occurrence frequencies from observational data, plus causal inference to tell a genuine drug effect from a confounded association. Neither existing tool has both ends.

Knowledge graphs organize qualitative knowledge well but carry no probabilistic or causal semantics. Structural causal models estimate intervention effects but sit disconnected from background knowledge, with graphs that are hand-crafted and don't scale. The authors want the two to merge, so the graph itself handles proposing hypotheses, finding confounders, and constraining the distribution.

The approach

Start with a formal definition of a Causal Knowledge Graph (CKG). On top of an ordinary KG it hangs a subset of relations marked causal (Rcausal), a function f mapping each node to a subset of the population, and a probability measure P over the population.

The key move is not equating the KG with the causal graph but making the causal structure a subset of it, so non-causal relations (like the ICD-10 is-a hierarchy) can still drive deductive reasoning and constrain the joint distribution (if u is-a v, any individual in u is in v).

From this they build a Drug-Disease CKG integrating four edge types: disease progression, 7,586 edges (a text-mined causal disease DAG); indications, 20,955 edges (MEDI-C); side effects, 59,119 edges (OnSIDES); and the ICD-10 hierarchy. The causal subset is disease-progression edges plus the inverse of indication edges (having a disease leads to a prescription).

Hypotheses are generated like this.

For each pair x causes-onset y, query x's indicated drug M and assemble the mediation chain "x (indication) → prescribe M → y (potential side effect)." Candidates come from two sources: explicit disease-progression pairs in the CKG (causal set), and significant comorbid pairs in UK Biobank (Hidalgo's relative risk, 34,843 kept after BH correction, then oriented by diagnosis timing).

A SPARQL query then drops hypotheses the graph already explains otherwise, the drug being indicated for both diseases, or both diseases being side effects of the same drug.

That leaves 12,561 causal and 81,610 comorbidity hypotheses.

Statistically it's causal mediation analysis estimating the natural indirect effect (R's mediation package, i.e. ACME), identified under sequential ignorability.

Confounder control is the heaviest part of the paper: beyond demographic and socioeconomic covariates, DAGitty runs the backdoor and disjunctive-cause criteria on the CKG's causal subgraph to pick adjustment sets; comorbidity count and drug count proxy for disease severity; and two LASSOs (one for the indication, one for the outcome) are unioned to shrink the covariate set, with λ from ten-fold CV.

Estimation uses 1,000 quasi-Bayesian simulations, HC standard errors, BH correction.

Key results

On the causal set, "disjunctive cause + LASSO" does best: precision 0.907, recall 0.282, F1 0.431 (versus backdoor+LASSO at F1 0.371). That high precision is partly a freebie, since those edges are literature-backed and mostly already-known effects.

The comorbidity set buys coverage instead: 30,724 significant positive ACMEs, the same recall but precision down to 0.749, more potentially novel candidates. Against Ryan et al.'s expert-curated set (56 overlapping pairs), the comorbidity set hits 70.0% precision and 60.6% recall.

External validation runs through drug repurposing: use predicted side effects to compute drug-drug similarity, then test whether similar drugs share indications. Predicted effects alone give ROCAUC 0.604, below OnSIDES alone at 0.620; but combining the two reaches 0.632 (p≪0.001), so the newly surfaced effects carry information rather than noise.

How solid is the evidence

The crux is sequential ignorability, mediation analysis requires that, after adjusting for confounders, both treatment and mediator are conditionally independent of the potential outcomes, which is untestable in observational data.

The authors admit the worst leak is disease severity: of 900 manually reviewed false positives, about 6% are actually disease progression rather than drug mediation. The mechanism is that a drug is given for an uncontrolled, worsening condition; mild responsive cases never develop the outcome, so the drug "appears" to mediate.

Using comorbidity and drug counts as severity proxies is a blunt instrument, weaker than a weighted index like Charlson. Measurement bias is real too: UKB medication use is self-reported without accurate timing or dose, and ICD diagnoses capture only clinically confirmed phenotypes, so mild or transient effects are invisible.

Both cohorts skew older and socially advantaged, so transportability is doubtful; and with a small, low-overlap gold-standard set, the reported high precision holds only on that small matched slice.
