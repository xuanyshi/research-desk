GLP-1 Receptor Agonists and Cancer Risk in Adults With Obesity

Hao Dai, Yongqiu Li, Yao An Lee, Ying Lu et al.

JAMA Oncology 11(10):1186–1193, 2025CAS 1 TopJCR Q1IF 23.9

30-second read

GLP-1RA use was associated with lower overall cancer incidence, with a relatively consistent gynecologic-cancer signal; the kidney-cancer signal requires independent confirmation.

Question
Among adults with overweight or obesity who were eligible for anti-obesity medication, how was GLP-1RA initiation associated with the incidence of 14 cancers?
Design
OneFlorida+ electronic health records, 2014–2024; target trial emulation, new-user design, and 1:1 time-dependent propensity-score matching.
Evidence strength Moderate
What was assessed
The matched cohort of about 86,600 adults, incidence rates, HRs with 95% CIs, competing-risk analyses, and treatment-effect heterogeneity analyses.
Downgrade triggers
Residual confounding remains possible; no multiplicity adjustment was reported across 14 cancers; several CIs approach the null, and follow-up is short relative to cancer latency.
Impact on interpretation
The overall and gynecologic-cancer associations support further triangulation. Borderline site-specific findings and the kidney-cancer signal should not yet be treated as established causal effects.

Reading adviceRead the design and limitations closely. Treat the site-specific results as a signal map rather than a basis for clinical prevention decisions.

Study design at a glance

Full deep read

Among obese/overweight adults eligible for anti-obesity drugs, GLP-1RA users had about one-sixth lower overall cancer incidence than nonusers (HR 0.83); endometrial, ovarian, and meningioma fell the most, while kidney cancer showed a rising signal.

The authors

The lead and corresponding author is Jiang Bian (Department of Biostatistics and Health Data Science, Indiana University School of Medicine, and the Regenstrief Institute; previously at the University of Florida), whose work focuses on health informatics and real-world data. Hao Dai and Yongqiu Li are co–first authors.

The group builds and uses the OneFlorida+ Clinical Research Network, a PCORnet regional data network, combining EHR pharmacoepidemiology with AI and informatics methods. Co-authors include pharmacoepidemiologists Yi Guo and Jingchuan Guo, oncologist Thomas J. George, and metabolism and biochemistry researcher Ramon C. Sun.

The abstract was presented at the 2025 ASCO Annual Meeting, and the full paper appeared in JAMA Oncology.

The problem

GLP-1RAs, including semaglutide, liraglutide, and tirzepatide, began as glucose-lowering drugs and have been approved for chronic weight management since 2014. Their long-term association with cancer incidence remains unsettled as prescribing expands.

Previous studies were largely confined to people with type 2 diabetes and compared GLP-1RAs with other glucose-lowering drugs. They could not directly answer what happens among the broader population eligible for anti-obesity medication, with or without diabetes, relative to nonuse. Most studies also did not examine heterogeneity of treatment effects.

The remaining gap was a design that covered the real prescribing population while controlling confounding and immortal time bias.

The approach

The study used target trial emulation: specify the eligibility criteria, treatment strategies, outcomes, and time zero of an ideal randomized trial, then approximate that trial with observational data to reduce confounding and immortal time bias. OneFlorida+ supplied longitudinal EHRs for about 20 million people across 14 health-care organizations from 2014 to 2024.

Eligible adults had BMI ≥30, or BMI 27–29.9 with at least one weight-related comorbidity. The study excluded people younger than 18, those with previous cancer, and those pregnant at entry.

Exposure was a first GLP-1RA prescription identified through RxNorm. Contemporaneous nonusers were aligned with users within a ±1-week window so that both groups had comparable index dates.

The analysis used 1:1 time-dependent propensity-score matching on demographic characteristics, three-year comorbidity history, one-year medication history, and laboratory measurements. Covariate balance was assessed with standardized mean differences, using SMD<0.1 as the target. An SMD at or above 0.1 would indicate residual imbalance and weaken interpretation of subsequent risk comparisons.

Outcomes were the incidence of 14 cancers, comprising 13 obesity-associated cancers and lung cancer. Cox models estimated HRs, and Fine–Gray models addressed death as a competing risk. A doubly robust meta-learner with triple cross-fitting estimated individualized treatment effects, followed by SHAP values and a single decision tree to identify potential benefit and harm subgroups.

Key results

After matching, the cohort contained 86,632 adults: 43,317 GLP-1RA users and 43,315 nonusers. Mean age was 52 years, 68% were women, about half had type 2 diabetes, and nearly half had BMI ≥30.

The pooled incidence across 14 cancers was 13.6 versus 16.4 per 1,000 person-years, with an overall HR of 0.83 (95% CI, 0.76–0.91; P=.002). This supports a lower overall incidence among GLP-1RA users, while remaining an observational association.

Site-specific reductions were reported for endometrial cancer (HR 0.75; 95% CI, 0.57–0.99), ovarian cancer (HR 0.53; 95% CI, 0.29–0.96), and meningioma (HR 0.69; 95% CI, 0.48–0.97). The composite gynecologic endpoint was more stable, with an HR of 0.68 (95% CI, 0.52–0.87).

Kidney cancer showed an elevated signal, with an HR of 1.38 (95% CI, 0.99–1.93), particularly among people younger than 65 or those with overweight rather than obesity. Because the CI crosses the null, independent confirmation is required.

High-ranking SHAP features for the kidney-cancer ITE included heart failure, metformin, insulin, proton-pump inhibitors, and substance use. Fine–Gray results were directionally consistent with the primary analysis.

How solid is the evidence

Target trial emulation, a new-user design, propensity-score matching, and competing-risk analysis all strengthen the primary comparison. They cannot eliminate every source of bias in an observational study.

The first limitation is confounding by indication and unmeasured confounding. Prescribing is related to health behavior, health-care intensity, and ability to pay, which EHR data may not capture fully.

BMI and glycemic control were not modeled longitudinally in a way that separates the drug from weight loss or improved glycemia. Because weight loss and glycemia may be mediators, routine covariate adjustment could also introduce overadjustment.

The second limitation is multiplicity. The three significant site-specific results have P values around .04–.05 and CI upper bounds of 0.96–0.99, with no reported multiplicity adjustment. The composite gynecologic endpoint is more stable than the borderline single-cancer estimates.

Rare-cancer event counts were small, CIs were wide, and follow-up was short relative to tumor latency. These results are therefore best treated as signals for further validation rather than established causal effects.
