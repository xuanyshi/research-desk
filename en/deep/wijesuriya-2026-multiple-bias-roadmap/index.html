Roadmap for systematic identification and analysis of multiple biases in causal inference

Rushani Wijesuriya, Rachael Hughes, John Carlin, Rachel L Peters et al.

American Journal of EpidemiologyCAS 2JCR Q1IF 4.1

30-second read

Start with an ideal trial and DAG to expose assumptions, then model unmeasured variables, mismeasurement, and selection jointly. In simulation, relative bias is 5.3% for simultaneous analysis versus 52.8% for the primary analysis, and above 20% for every one-at-a-time analysis.

Question
When selection, confounding, and measurement biases coexist, how can they be analyzed jointly from explicit assumptions?
Design
A three-step methodological roadmap illustrated with a breastfeeding–asthma analysis of 5,279 HealthNuts infants and 1,000 simulated datasets comparing simultaneous with one-at-a-time bias analysis.
Evidence strength Moderate
What was assessed
Ideal-trial components, primary and alternative DAGs, bias-parameter models in HealthNuts, 1,000 probabilistic draws and bootstraps, and a known-truth simulation.
Downgrade triggers
Validity depends on a correct alternative DAG, bias models, and external parameter distributions. Many parameters are unidentified by the primary data, case-study estimates differ little, and the simulation is simplified.
Impact on interpretation
Separate sensitivity analyses can mislead when biases are dependent or oppose one another. Joint results remain sensitivity ranges conditional on model and parameter choices.

Multiple quantitative-bias-analysis roadmap

04

Full deep read

Use an ideal trial and a DAG to expose assumptions first, then put selection, confounding, and measurement bias into one quantitative model; separate sensitivity analyses can misstate their joint impact.

The authors

The work is led by Rushani Wijesuriya and Margarita Moreno-Betancur's Clinical Epidemiology & Biostatistics Unit at the Murdoch Children's Research Institute, with collaborators at the University of Melbourne, the University of Bristol's MRC Integrative Epidemiology Unit, and the University of Queensland.

It was published online in the American Journal of Epidemiology on 16 July 2026 and is currently an accepted manuscript.

The problem

Observational causal analyses simultaneously depend on exchangeability, consistency, positivity, and measurement assumptions. Quantitative bias analyses often choose one violation to quantify while leaving the rest embedded in the primary analysis. Even a series of separate analyses assumes, each time, that the other biases are absent.

That is a poor guide to the combined effect when biases are dependent or point in opposite directions.

The approach

The roadmap has three steps. First, define the ideal randomized trial through its eligibility criteria, treatment strategies, assignment, follow-up, and outcome measurement. For each component, compare the observational emulation with the ideal and state the identification assumptions required to bridge the gap, drawing them in a primary-analysis DAG where possible.

Second, add substantively plausible violations to an alternative DAG. The HealthNuts example asks for the causal effect of any breastfeeding in the first 12 months on asthma risk at age six among 5,279 infants.

Its alternative graph adds generalizability bias from restricting participation by English ability and consent, collider selection from outcome loss to follow-up, unmeasured confounding by gestational hypertension, and exposure/outcome error from parental reports.

Third, target the ideal joint distribution containing the true exposure and outcome, an unmeasured confounder, and the selection process. When the alternative graph implies dependence among these variables, the trigger for joint analysis is met.

The authors factor a bias-adjusting probability according to the graph, specify logistic bias models, sample the true exposure, outcome, and unmeasured confounder, derive inverse-probability selection weights, and fit a weighted outcome regression.

Each Monte Carlo draw yields one effect estimate adjusted for all represented biases. Distributions over bias parameters and bootstrap resampling propagate parameter uncertainty and sampling error into the interval.

Key results

The case study used 1,000 probabilistic draws and 1,000 bootstraps. The jointly adjusted breastfeeding risk difference was about -0.10 versus roughly -0.05 in the primary analysis; the intervals overlapped considerably, so the authors describe only a somewhat stronger protective association. The proof-of-concept simulation is sharper: 1,000 datasets of n=2,000 with a true risk ratio of 0.51.

Simultaneous analysis produced a mean log risk ratio of -0.70, 5.3% relative bias, and 0.92 coverage. The primary analysis averaged -0.32 with 52.8% relative bias. Every one-at-a-time analysis had more than 20% relative bias; outcome-measurement correction alone even reversed the estimate to 2.60 on the log scale.

How solid is the evidence

The paper completes the whole chain from assumption inventory through an alternative DAG to joint analysis, and releases its code. Its simulation criterion is concrete: compare estimates with the known true log risk ratio; below 10% relative bias the joint method is approximately unbiased, while above 20% the one-at-a-time methods materially miss the target.

The result is conditional on a correct alternative DAG, correct bias models, and defensible parameter distributions.

Those parameters are not identified by the primary data; some conditional parameters are approximated with marginal literature values and others rely on expert judgment. The simulation covers one simplified graph with binary variables. DAGs also cannot display every form of measurement or generalizability bias.

Joint and separate estimates barely differed in the applied example, so the advantage still needs testing under more structures, model misspecification, and independent real-data applications. The accepted manuscript may change during copyediting or typesetting.
