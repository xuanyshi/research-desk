Evaluating Directed Acyclic Graphs with DAGMetrics: Insights from Tuber and Soil Microbiome Data

Pavel Averin, Ifigeneia Mellidou, Maria Ganopoulou, Aliki Xanthopoulou et al.

Agronomy 15 (2025):987

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ DAGMetricsCausal discoveryGraph comparisonMarkov blanketMicrobiomeR software

Question
Without a ground-truth DAG, how can learned graphs be compared for complexity, similarity, stability, and local structure?
Design
An R package and case study generating 360 DAGs from tuber and soil microbiomes across four algorithms, tests or scores, scaling options, and transformations, followed by Markov-blanket analysis.
Evidence strength Low to moderate
What was assessed
Edges, colliders, isolated nodes, graph-to-graph F1, and Markov blankets for 360 learned DAGs from two microbiome datasets.
Downgrade triggers
Moderate complexity and stability across scaling and transformations trigger candidate selection; shared algorithmic assumptions can make stability preserve systematic error.
Impact on interpretation
The package suits structural quality control after discovery. Without ground truth, intervention, or external validation, it cannot confirm causal edges.

Comparing DAGs without ground truth

Full deep read

DAGMetrics compares complexity and stability among learned DAGs; without a ground-truth graph, these metrics select structures but cannot confirm causal direction.

The authors

Pavel Averin and Theodoros Moysiadis are affiliated with the University of Nicosia; Ifigeneia Mellidou and Aliki Xanthopoulou with Greece's ELGO-DIMITRA Institute of Plant Breeding and Genetic Resources; Maria Ganopoulou with Aristotle University of Thessaloniki. The team spans computer science, plant research, and microbiome analysis.

The problem

Causal-discovery outputs can change with tests, scores, scaling, and transformations. Real microbiome datasets rarely have a known DAG, leaving researchers unsure which graph merits interpretation. DAGMetrics provides one interface for global and local structural comparisons.

The approach

The R package reports edges, colliders, and isolated nodes, compares graphs using F1 and shared-edge counts, and visualizes Markov blankets. On potato tuber and soil microbiomes, the authors compare PC, Inter-IAMB, Tabu Search, and Hill-Climbing with Kendall, Spearman, or Pearson tests, AIC or BIC scores, three scaling options, and two transformations.

The analysis produces 240 tuber DAGs and 120 soil DAGs. The selection object is graph structure across configurations. Moderate complexity and stability across preprocessing trigger selection; the practical effect is choosing candidate graphs for Markov-blanket and biological interpretation.

Key results

Score-based methods generally produce denser graphs, while constraint-based methods produce more isolated nodes. PC and Inter-IAMB with Spearman or Kendall are relatively stable across scaling and transformations. PC with Spearman is favored as a compromise between complexity and stability.

Tuber Markov blankets contain up to seven nodes and soil blankets up to four, with many isolated nodes. Some edges recur across terroirs and storage stages and align with known microbial ecology. Outputs from different algorithm families remain highly dissimilar.

How solid is the evidence

Two real datasets and 360 graphs demonstrate software function and structural sensitivity. There is no ground-truth DAG, so F1 measures agreement between estimated graphs, not accuracy. A stable error scores well, and shared assumptions can make algorithms jointly miss latent variables, compositional constraints, or batch effects.

Microbial abundance data are high-dimensional, small-sample, and compositional, which can distort standard conditional-independence tests. Biological interpretation is largely post hoc, without intervention or independent-cohort validation.
