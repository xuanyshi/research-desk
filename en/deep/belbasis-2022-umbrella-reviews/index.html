Full deep read

A sound umbrella review places multiple meta-analyses under one re-analysis and grading protocol; it provides a landscape and priorities but cannot repair shared upstream bias.

The authors

Lazaros Belbasis, Vanesa Bellou, and John P A Ioannidis have extensive experience in meta-research and evidence credibility. Their affiliations span meta-research groups in Berlin, Ioannina, and Stanford. This is a BMJ Medicine research methods and reporting article.

The problem

When many systematic reviews already exist for one disease, another single-factor review cannot answer which interventions or risk factors have the strongest evidence. Umbrella reviews compare many questions, but overlapping reviews, inconsistent statistics, and secondary-data errors can compound bias. This article defines when and how to conduct one.

The approach

Four stages anchor the workflow: search and selection, data extraction, standardized re-analysis and grading, and interpretation. Eligibility specifies population, intervention or exposure, comparator, and outcome; two researchers screen and extract independently. Overlapping reviews may be prioritized by recency, study count, prospective evidence, or quality, with exclusions reported.

Investigators should extract study-specific effects, 95% CIs, sample sizes, and event counts, then rerun every meta-analysis under one protocol. GRADE can address interventions. Epidemiological associations are judged by evidence volume, P value, heterogeneity, 95% prediction interval, small-study effects, and excess significance. Confounding, reverse causation, selection, and information bias are interpreted separately.

Key results

This is a methods guide, not a new clinical-effect study. Its working example is an umbrella review of non-genetic type 2 diabetes risk factors: 86 articles and 142 associations were included. Although 116 associations had P<0.05, only 11 associations met strong-evidence criteria combining evidence amount, significance, prediction intervals, heterogeneity, and bias signals.

The authors also cite an empirical assessment of 57 umbrella reviews and 3,744 observational meta-analyses, where the grading features contributed largely independent information. Box 3 specifies the required reporting set, including cases, sample size, model, pooled estimate, prediction interval, largest study, and bias tests.

How solid is the evidence

Methodological experience, empirical meta-research, and a worked example support process guidance, not any specific medical relationship. Umbrella reviews cover only topics already synthesized. Bias can accumulate across primary studies, meta-analyses, and the umbrella review itself, while complete verification against every original report is rarely feasible.

The object of grading is each meta-analysis; prespecified statistical and bias criteria trigger a tier; the practical effect is prioritization for further validation. Even the top tier needs design and biological assessment before a causal claim.
