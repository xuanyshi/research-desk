Secondhand smoke exposure and human health: an umbrella review

Shengxin Zhang, Shen Li, Xinggang Yang, Ziyang Cheng et al.

Nature Human Behaviour (2026)

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Umbrella reviewSecondhand smokeLung cancerCardiovascular diseasePerinatal outcomesEvidence grading

30-second read

Question
Across the life course, which health outcomes are linked to secondhand smoke, and which findings combine stronger statistical credibility with interpretable dose-response information?
Design
A preregistered umbrella review searched four databases through August 2025, assessed overlap among 299 eligible meta-analyses, and repooled 226 exposure-outcome associations from 111 systematic reviews.
Evidence strength Tiered; limited overall
What was assessed
Each meta-analysis for a specific secondhand-smoke setting and health outcome, including study and case counts, pooled effects, prediction intervals, heterogeneity, and bias signals.
Downgrade triggers
Class I requires >1,000 cases, random-effects P<10^-6, a prediction interval excluding the null, I²<50%, and no small-study effect or excess significance; Classes II-IV progressively relax criteria.
Impact on interpretation
Class I/II findings enter the priority evidence tier. When GRADE remains low or very low, the conclusion is a statistically credible association, not a confirmed causal effect.

From overlapping reviews to an interpretable evidence map

04

Full deep read

This umbrella review organizes secondhand-smoke evidence into 226 exposure-outcome associations. Lung cancer, cardiovascular disease, and selected perinatal outcomes have comparatively strong statistical credibility, while GRADE certainty remains low or very low for most results. The review does not establish that every reported association is causal.

The authors

Shengxin Zhang, Shen Li, Xinggang Yang, and colleagues are affiliated with West China Hospital of Sichuan University, the University of Electronic Science and Technology of China, and other institutions. Hu Liao, Juan Huang, and Xuelei Ma are corresponding authors. The paper appeared in Nature Human Behaviour in July 2026. Its protocol was registered in PROSPERO (CRD42024583424), and the data and analysis code are available through Zenodo.

The problem

Secondhand-smoke research spans pregnancy, childhood, and adulthood, with substantial variation in sources, measurements, and outcomes. Multiple systematic reviews may repeatedly include the same primary studies. The authors sought to rerun existing meta-analyses under one protocol and distinguish mature evidence, useful research gaps, and questions where adding similar observational studies may offer little information.

The exposure scope needs to be separated before interpretation. Environmental secondhand smoke in adults or children concerns non-active smokers exposed at home, work, or elsewhere. Prenatal maternal smoking treats the fetus as the passively exposed individual. Maternal active smoking, transplacental fetal exposure, and postnatal household exposure are different estimands.

The approach

Four databases were searched through 24 August 2025, yielding 5,405 records. Of 299 systematic reviews eligible for reanalysis, representative reviews were selected for each exposure-outcome combination by dose-response reporting, study count, methodological quality, and exposure detail. The final reanalysis used study-level data from 111 reviews.

Each association was repooled with a REML random-effects model. ORs, RRs, and HRs remained on their original scales. The authors assessed AMSTAR 2, GRADE, 95% prediction intervals, heterogeneity, small-study effects, excess significance, and Rosenthal fail-safe numbers. For non-significant results, scenario analyses estimated how many additional studies would be needed for significance or 80% power.

The object of evidence classification is one exposure-outcome meta-analysis. Class I is triggered by more than 1,000 cases, or more than 20,000 participants for continuous outcomes; random-effects P<10^-6; a prediction interval excluding the null; I²<50%; and no small-study effect or excess significance. Its practical effect is placement in the highest statistical-credibility tier. It does not remove residual confounding, exposure misclassification, or selection bias.

Key results

The reanalysis covered 111 systematic reviews, about 253.3 million participants, 130 health outcomes, and 226 independent associations. Eighty-seven outcomes had at least one significant association, and 28 outcome findings reached Class I or II. Every Class I/II result exceeded the fail-safe-number threshold of 5k+10, although that metric only counts hypothetical null-effect studies needed to change statistical significance.

For adult chronic disease, secondhand-smoke exposure was associated with lung cancer at RR 1.24 (95% CI 1.16–1.32; 82 studies; Class II; low GRADE certainty), overall cardiovascular disease at OR 1.24 (95% CI 1.16–1.31; 52 studies; Class II; very-low GRADE certainty), and stroke at RR 1.24 (95% CI 1.16–1.32; 38 studies; Class II). Lung cancer also showed dose-response patterns by intensity, duration, and cumulative exposure.

Cancer evidence varied sharply. Breast cancer had RR 1.25 (Class III), cervical cancer RR 1.53 (Class III), pancreatic cancer RR 1.20 (Class III), and colorectal cancer RR 1.17 (Class IV). Kidney and endometrial cancer estimates were not statistically significant. Beyond lung cancer, most cancer associations had very-low GRADE certainty or weaker statistical classes and are better treated as candidates for triangulation.

For early-life outcomes, prenatal maternal smoking of 11-19 cigarettes per day was associated with low birth weight at OR 2.05 (Class I), while prenatal maternal smoking was associated with perinatal death at RR 1.33 (Class II). These estimates concern fetal passive exposure to maternal active smoking and should not be transferred directly to environmental secondhand smoke among non-smoking pregnant women.

How solid is the evidence

Repooling study-level effects under one model reduces incomparability from copied review-level summaries. Overlap analysis, preregistration, duplicate extraction, and open data and code improve auditability. Reporting Evidence class beside GRADE also makes visible the gap between strong statistical signals and limited causal certainty.

All source evidence is observational. Residual active smoking, socioeconomic position, occupational exposure, and correlated behaviors can confound associations. Most studies measured exposure by questionnaire, leaving recall error and former-smoker misclassification. An umbrella review inherits selective reporting and bias from source reviews. Selecting one representative review reduces duplicate counting but may discard distinct time windows or population definitions.

The reported P values were not adjusted for multiple comparisons. A large fail-safe number assumes added studies have an average null effect and cannot rule out systematic bias. Prospective scenario analysis depends on assumed future effects and variances; it measures sensitivity to more studies of the same kind, not the value of information or causal identifiability.
