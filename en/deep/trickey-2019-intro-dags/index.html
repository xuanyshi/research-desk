Introduction to Causal Directed Acyclic Graphs

Amber W. Trickey

Stanford S-SPIRE Works in Progress (presentation), 2019-01-28

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Causal DAGConfounder adjustmentCollider biasTeaching deck

30-second read

Construct the DAG independently of available data and include common causes of any two variables in the graph. The adjustment set should block all backdoor paths without conditioning on colliders or their descendants.

Question
How can a clinical researcher build a DAG from a research question and use it to choose a minimally sufficient adjustment set?
Design
A 30-page teaching deck using nonoperative appendicitis management and 30-day emergency visits to move from exposure and outcome to mediators, modifiers, confounders, unmeasured common causes, and selection variables.
Evidence strength Limited
What was assessed
A four-step construction process, an appendicitis teaching example, and adjustment guidance adapted from the AHRQ CER User's Guide.
Downgrade triggers
This is introductory teaching material with no new dataset or methods comparison. Some simplified notation and terminology need support from formal causal-inference sources.
Impact on interpretation
The deck is useful as a construction and discussion checklist, but it cannot establish that a proposed graph is correct or replace sensitivity analysis and domain validation.

Reading adviceRead PDF pages 11–14 as one four-step sequence, then inspect the summary on page 27. Focus on omitted common causes, selection variables, and unmeasured variables.

Full deep read

A Stanford intro-to-DAGs teaching deck: confounding, colliders, mediators, backdoor paths, minimal adjustment sets, all done cleanly and to standard.

The authors

A solo teaching presentation by Amber W. Trickey, senior biostatistician at Stanford's S-SPIRE (Surgery Policy Improvement Research and Education Center), where she supports surgical and health-services research teams in study design and analysis.

The problem

Up front: this is a slide deck, not a research paper. The author is a senior biostatistician at Stanford S-SPIRE (Surgery Policy Improvement Research and Education), presenting in January 2019 to the group's Works in Progress for surgical and health-services researchers — clinicians and statisticians.

Their data is almost entirely observational (Medicare, HCUP, NSQIP, Optum); treatment isn't randomized, it's set by the often-unknown mechanisms of routine care.

The teaching problem is plain: when you can't randomize and correlation ≠ causation, how do you decide in a principled way which variables to adjust for. The answer is the DAG — draw your causal assumptions, then use the picture to see where bias comes from, whether the effect is identifiable, and which covariates to pick.

The approach

The content follows a standard arc. Motivation first: DAGs display sources of bias, decide whether an effect is identifiable from the available data, and make assumptions explicit (the author is honest that analytic models carry assumptions anyway — the DAG just surfaces them).

Then the rules: directed and acyclic, time flowing left to right, A for exposure, Y for outcome, C for covariates, U for unmeasured, with parent/child/ancestor/descendant defined one by one.

The core is a four-step build:

  • Step 1, articulate the research question and place A, Y, and a "?"; step 2, consider the mediators and moderators embedded in it;
  • step 3, add confounders (common causes of A and Y); step 4, fill in other relevant variables.

The inclusion rule: every common cause of any two nodes goes in, including unmeasured ones and selection variables; variables causing Y but not A are optional.

Then adjustment — backdoor paths, colliders (two arrows colliding at a common descendant), and the three iron rules of conditioning: conditioning on a variable in an open backdoor path removes the non-causal association, conditioning on a collider instead opens the path it was blocking, and conditioning on a mediator strips out part of the real effect.

It closes by folding confounding, collider, and selection bias (including Berkson's) into one structural family, points to DAGitty, and ends on the AHRQ Sauer-VanderWeele summary: build the DAG independent of the available data, include all common causes, and find a minimal set that blocks every backdoor without inadvertently opening a collider.

Key results

It's teaching material, so the "results" are the rules and examples it lands. The three conditioning rules are stated cleanly — the most valuable slide in the deck.

The appendicitis example is from the author's own JAMA Surgery paper (A = nonoperative management, Y = 30-day ED visits, confounders age/gender/insurance/severity/language, income unmeasured): a clean pure-confounding case with no mediators, moderators, or colliders, where conditioning still leaves residual confounding from language and income.

The low-back-pain surgery example exists to demonstrate a collider: conditioning on "surgery," a common effect, opens collider bias. One positive, one cautionary — exactly the point that you don't dump every variable into the regression. The tool is DAGitty; the closing principle is the minimal adjustment set.

How solid is the evidence

It isn't evidence, it's a lecture, so the only fair questions are accuracy, clarity, and coverage.

Accuracy is fine and current: treating selection bias as a special case of collider bias (the Hernán/Greenland structural view) is right, the three conditioning rules are exact, the "build the DAG independent of the data" point is the correct emphasis, and it even flags that "adjusting/controlling" is misleading language and one should say "conditioning" (Shrier-Platt).

The weakness is coverage, which is what matters for a reader like me. No formal definition of d-separation, no named backdoor criterion, no do-calculus or front-door, and IV appears only as notation.

The deck teaches colliders but does not cover M-bias or butterfly bias, a common adjustment trap. Time-varying confounding and g-methods are also outside its scope. It acknowledges disagreement over whether effect modifiers belong in a DAG but leaves the issue unresolved.

DAGitty's algorithmic capabilities, including minimal sufficient sets and testable implications, are compressed into a brief instruction to save the code. As a 2019 introduction, the material is sound but stops before the harder methodological problems.
