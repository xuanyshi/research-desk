Triangulation in aetiological epidemiology

Debbie A. Lawlor, Kate Tilling, George Davey Smith

International Journal of EpidemiologyCAS 2 TopJCR Q1IF 5.7

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Causal inferenceTriangulationDirection of biasMendelian randomization

30-second read

At least two approaches must address the same underlying causal question while having different and unrelated key bias sources. Exposure duration and timing, bias sources, and likely bias directions must also be explicit.

Question
When do multiple epidemiologic results constitute triangulation rather than repetition of a shared bias?
Design
A methodological framework proposing five minimum criteria, illustrated with LDL-C and coronary disease, systolic blood pressure and coronary disease, maternal glucose and birthweight, and breastfeeding and childhood BMI.
Evidence strength Moderate
What was assessed
Five minimum criteria, the method–assumption–bias map in Table 1, RCT and MR instrumental-variable DAGs in Figure 1, and three empirical triangulation examples.
Downgrade triggers
The framework is mainly qualitative. Judgments about whether biases are related and their directions require subject knowledge, and the paper offers no general quantitative pooling method.
Impact on interpretation
Convergence strengthens causal interpretation when the criteria are met and biases differ in direction. Statistical consistency or poolability is not required.

Full deep read

Triangulation isn't repeating or pooling the same design — it's cross-checking approaches whose key biases are unrelated and ideally point in opposite directions; if they still converge, the causal claim is on firmer ground.

The authors

All three authors are at the MRC Integrative Epidemiology Unit, University of Bristol, known for causal-inference methods in aetiological epidemiology. Lead author Deborah Lawlor is its deputy director, Kate Tilling a professor of medical statistics, and senior author George Davey Smith the unit's director and a Mendelian-randomization pioneer.

The problem

Aetiological epidemiology asks "what causes disease," yet many exposures (breastfeeding, alcohol, the long-term effects of smoking) can't be randomized.

Every single approach carries its own bias; repeating one design, or pooling it by meta-analysis, quietly assumes all the estimates are unbiased and estimating the same effect — but if they're all pulled the same way by the same residual confounding, the "consistent answer" is very likely consistently wrong.

In the authors' words, that similar answer "is likely to be the (residually) confounded answer." What's needed is a framework that systematically exploits the fact that different approaches have different biases, rather than manufacturing false agreement by repetition.

The approach

Their definition, near-verbatim: strengthening causal inference by integrating results from several different "approaches" whose key sources of potential bias differ and are assumed to be largely unrelated. The word "approaches" (not "designs") is deliberate — comparisons can be across designs (RCT vs cohort) and across analytic strategies within one cohort (negative controls, cross-context comparison, MR).

The name comes from 1600s surveying: compute a hard-to-measure distance from a few easy ones plus the geometry of triangles; here the hard-to-measure quantity is an unbiased causal effect, and the "different locations" are approaches with different biases.

The core is a minimum set of five criteria: (1) at least two — ideally more — approaches with differing, unrelated key bias sources are compared; (2) they address the same underlying causal question; (3) the duration and timing of exposure each approach captures is taken into account when comparing; (4) each approach's key bias sources are made explicit; (5) the expected direction of each bias is stated where feasible, ideally with some approaches biased in opposite directions.

Criterion 5 is the crux: if approaches biased in different directions still converge, bias can't easily explain the agreement. A key boundary — throwing many estimators (stratification, PS, IPW, g-formula; or IV, matching, DID) at the same dataset is NOT triangulation, because they share the same measurement error and residual confounding.

What counts as a "bias source" is mechanistic: confounding in observational regression comes from unmeasured confounders, "confounding" in an RCT from subversion of randomization, in MR from population stratification — the same confounding construct, three unrelated sources, so triangulating them is legitimate.

Table 1 lays out each design's assumptions and key bias sources as a knowledge table; Figure 1 uses six IV-DAGs for LDLc→CHD to show that MR on HMGCR variants is mimicking statins, so its pleiotropy path is shared with a statin RCT — those two should not be triangulated, whereas variants acting on a different path give an unrelated violation.

Key results

A methods/opinion piece — the output is a framework, not numbers. The most operationalizable artifacts are Table 1 (design → assumptions → key bias sources) and Figure 1's DAG-based judgment.

The authors are blunt that triangulation today "will mostly provide a qualitative assessment," and that "pointing to the same conclusion does not mean that the results are statistically consistent and could be pooled."

Three deliberately messy examples show the use. Lower SBP→CHD estimates from MR, cohort meta-analysis, and IV ratios from RCTs agree in direction but differ in magnitude; plotting them against exposure duration supports a cumulative-exposure interpretation. Breastfeeding→BMI evidence, including bespoke ALSPAC negative-control outcomes, points toward no causal effect and residual confounding. Maternal glucose→birthweight does not reconcile and ends at "more evidence needed."

How solid is the evidence

The authors are honest about the limits they draw. First, it's qualitative only, with no pooling method — "we have not considered how to pool data in a triangulation framework," and deriving "plausible bounds of causal effect" is explicitly future work.

Second, "are the bias sources unrelated?" is a judgment call that can be wrong — the HMGCR/statin case shows two nominally different designs can share the same bias source.

Third, the direction of bias often can't be predicted a priori; it "cannot be predicted in general," and new biases may only surface once analysis begins. Fourth, they don't claim novelty (it shares roots with Hill's consistency and responses to Duhem–Quine); the contribution is the definition, criteria, and demonstrations.

So it's a program, not a runnable inference engine — and the harder the case, the more it defers to "further evidence."
