Causal inference on human behaviour

Drew H. Bailey, Konrad P. Kording, Julia M. Rohrer, Kou Murayama

Nature Human Behaviour

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Causal inferenceTriangulationBehavioural scienceConfounding & colliders

30-second read

Causal analysis begins by specifying the intervention, outcome, time window, target population, and estimand. No single advanced model simultaneously resolves confounding, heterogeneity, interference, and measurement; cross-design comparison is more informative.

Question
For human behaviours that are hard to manipulate, heterogeneous, and time-varying, how should researchers define identifiable causal questions and combine experimental with observational evidence?
Design
A cross-disciplinary narrative review. Boxes 2–8 cover question definition, under-control, over-control, effect heterogeneity, interference, timescales, and complex interventions, followed by a triangulation framework across experimental and observational work.
Evidence strength Conceptual
What was assessed
Seven conceptual and methodological challenges, examples from behavioural science, longitudinal-method suggestions, and the experiment–observation–theory loop in Figure 1.
Downgrade triggers
This is a non-technical review without a systematic search, quantitative synthesis, or prospective validation of the triangulation workflow. Each recommendation depends on the estimand and identification assumptions.
Impact on interpretation
The paper works as a design and analysis checklist. It cannot assign a universal causal-evidence grade to a behavioural exposure.

Reading adviceBegin with Box 2, then select the relevant failure modes in Boxes 3–8. Finish with Figure 1 and ask whether the designs estimate comparable effects.

Behavioural causal-research pathway

04

Full deep read

A Nature Human Behaviour conceptual overview: it sorts the difficulties of causal inference on behaviour into seven challenges, notes that fixing one usually worsens another, and lands its hopes on triangulation plus asking sharper questions.

The authors

An interdisciplinary group led by Drew H. Bailey of UC Irvine's School of Education, with senior author Kou Murayama, an educational psychologist at the University of Tübingen. Co-authors include Konrad Kording (Penn) and Julia Rohrer, who studies causal inference in psychology.

The problem

Behavioural science asks "what causes what," but behaviour sits inside a complex causal system we observe only in part. Variables like happiness or cognitive skill mostly can't be randomized, every method carries its own bias, and in several fields (psychology especially) researchers often haven't settled whether they even want a causal effect.

The authors' read: causal-inference tools have advanced fast and longitudinal data are more available, but the advances are scattered across economics, epidemiology, political science, psychology and sociology, each patching its own corner, with no single map that lays the challenges and the ways out side by side. This Review is that map — non-technical, cross-disciplinary, focused on longitudinal data.

The approach

The framework comes in two halves.

First, seven challenges, named as in the paper. (1) Questions that aren't specific enough: "why are some people happier" is a reverse causal question, "does happiness predict health" is only prediction, and only "the returns to an extra year of schooling" is both specific and forward-causal.

(2) Under-control: you can essentially never prove all confounders have been adjusted for. (3) Over-control: conditioning on a mediator or collider introduces post-treatment bias — in police-stop data, "being stopped" is a collider that makes racial discrimination look smaller.

(4) Effect heterogeneity: an average treatment effect hides variation, and under some conditions the average itself is biased. (5) Interference/spillover: peers and siblings affect each other, breaking SUTVA. (6) Timescales: effects have latency and may be non-stationary.

(7) "Fat-handed" interventions: you can't nudge only happiness without also moving mood, and behavioural constructs are often ill-defined and not directly manipulable.

The through-line: a method optimized to fix one problem usually worsens another — add controls to fight under-control and you risk over-control. No free lunch.

The second half is the way out, four strategies: clarify the question and usually narrow it; once it's set, choose the design and variables and mind construct measurement; treat "causal informativeness" as a continuum rather than a binary switch, instead of refusing to say "causal" just because the data are observational; and triangulate (Fig.

1) — put the strong estimate from a well-defined but probably fat-handed intervention next to the observational estimate, read convergence or divergence against theory, and revise theory, using within-study experimental-versus-observational comparison, coherent pattern matching, robustness and falsification tests, bounding/sensitivity analysis, and structural models.

Key results

A methods/opinion piece — the output is a taxonomy and recommendations, not numbers. The useful artifacts are the seven-challenge list, the four strategies, and the Fig. 1 loop where intervention estimates, observational estimates and theory mutually check and revise one another; Boxes 2–8 pair each challenge with tentative solutions and further reading.

A few concrete claims worth keeping: state the study's goal (causal estimation) and the certainty a method affords separately, rather than lumping both under "causal"; and structural models get a positive example — Todd and Wolpin's model of school attendance predicted how much a subsidy would raise attendance, later confirmed by a randomized experiment.

The authors are blunt that triangulation won't cleanly answer the broad question, but can answer narrower ones more solidly.

How solid is the evidence

As a Review it's honest but limited. No new method, no data, no validation; the diagnosis is sharp, but the prescriptions are mostly tools the field already has, labelled "tentative." Its triangulation is a reasoning stance, not an algorithm: no criterion for how much convergence counts, nothing on how to weight several biased estimates.

Worse, whether two approaches' biases point the same way or opposite is itself a judgment call — the authors themselves warn that if controls are poor and interventions all fat-handed, the net bias of both may go the same direction, and then agreement stops being evidence. On DAGs, good/bad controls and colliders, this is textbook for anyone who knows causal graphs.
