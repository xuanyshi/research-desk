Living-DAG lifecycle

Full deep read

DAGs today are used as one-off, study-specific tools and then thrown away. The author argues for turning them into "living," shared causal infrastructure: graded by levels of evidence, versioned, revised as evidence arrives, and built collaboratively, so science can accumulate.

The authors

Robert J. Reynolds is a data scientist for NASA Johnson Space Center's Human Health and Performance Directorate (via contractor KBR) and network scientist for its Human System Risk Board. His work applies causal diagrams and epidemiology to astronaut health risk.

The problem

Over two decades DAGs went from novelty to standard method in epidemiology, used to frame the question, clarify the estimand, find sources of bias, and design the analysis. But the common workflow wastes them: assume the DAG structure is correct (from prior knowledge or the literature), extract the adjustment set, then discard the DAG.

It may appear in a paper, but it is rarely subjected to network analysis beyond d-separation and almost never linked to prior evidence or competing models.

The DAG becomes a study-specific instrument that turns irrelevant once the study ends. The author's point is that this throws away the DAG's potential to support cumulative science.

The approach

This is an opinion / methods call, not an empirical study. The core proposal is to treat DAGs as epistemic infrastructure. Living DAGs are not meant to replace the study-specific DAGs used for individual analyses; they are a shared, domain-level causal foundation from which researchers derive their own study-specific DAGs (adding selection nodes, measurement specs, timing) before analysis.

A living DAG has four features: each causal relationship is annotated with a level of evidence (speculative/weak/moderate/strong); it is updated over time as new studies, experiments, or mechanistic models arrive; it is versioned, so you can trace how understanding evolved and why; and it is modular, so parts can be evaluated, revised, or reused independently.

Context differences are handled not by building a separate graph per setting but with modular modifying-factor nodes (geographic, temporal, or demographic nodes that shift pathway strength).

The author separates the universal causal mechanism (the domain DAG) from context-specific magnitudes such as exposure windows: air pollution from three months ago cannot plausibly cause lung cancer today, but exposure from 10 to 20 years ago can; the domain DAG encodes the mechanism, the study-specific DAG specifies the window.

A development-and-revision cycle is sketched (review, propose changes, ratify, release a new version), with competing DAGs allowed to coexist.

Key results

Being an opinion piece, it offers no experimental numbers but two examples already in progress. NASA models human-spaceflight risk with 29 interlinked DAGs, each mapping one risk monitored by the Human System Risk Board, combined into the NASA Human System Risk Network (HSRN), built through structured expert elicitation plus a configuration-review process.

An international cerebral-palsy consortium of more than 50 researchers and clinicians, with input from caregivers and patients, spent two years iteratively building a CP DAG.

Both started from structured expert elicitation and were refined repeatedly. The proposal grew out of a workshop at the 2025 SER meeting, with three related sessions accepted for 2026.

How solid is the evidence

This is a call to action, not a validated method. Its "evidence" is the NASA and CP cases, both products of expert consensus, with no quantification of how much a living DAG improves on the study-specific workflow.

Several hard problems are named but not solved: how to grade evidence levels objectively (who sets the speculative-to-strong boundaries), how to adjudicate competing DAGs, who governs versioning, and how to align incentives and ownership across institutions.

The air-pollution example is intuitive, but the claim that mechanisms are universal while magnitudes are context-specific itself needs empirical support. As a manifesto it is persuasive; as a method it still lacks formalism and validation.
