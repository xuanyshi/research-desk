A Pilot Project Leveraging Large Language Models for Automated Screening and Variable Extraction in Observational Studies

Manjil M. Pradhan, Melissa L. Rethlefsen, Scott A. Malec

medRxiv

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Large language modelsSystematic review automationConfounder adjustmentInformation extraction

30-second read

VarEx reaches 0.80 precision and 0.79 recall in its main reference set. LitScreen recall is 1.00 in four SYNERGY datasets and 0.725 in another, but production corpora lack complete inclusion gold standards and the manuscript retains placeholder content.

Question
Can open-source LLMs screen observational studies with high recall and extract exposures, outcomes, and covariates with an audit trail?
Design
A medRxiv preprint combining LitScreen for abstract and full-text RAG screening with VarEx for role-specific extraction and mapping to ten Metaconfoundr categories.
Evidence strength Limited
What was assessed
A 20-paper hypertension–ADRD set with 240 reference covariates, several SYNERGY screening benchmarks, verbatim evidence gates, and processing time per paper.
Downgrade triggers
The preprint is not peer reviewed. Production corpora report expert-reviewed precision without complete recall, and the screening appendix and main text still contain unfinished markers.
Impact on interpretation
The results support an auditable high-recall screening and extraction prototype, but do not show that it can replace dual human review without supervision.

Auditable screening and variable extraction

Full deep read

Two open-source LLM pipelines: LitScreen screens observational studies, while VarEx extracts exposures, outcomes, and covariates and aligns them to Metaconfoundr. Screening recall is 1.00 in four of five SYNERGY datasets, but production corpora lack complete inclusion gold standards and the manuscript still contains placeholders.

The authors

From the University of New Mexico's Division of Translational Informatics. Senior author Scott A. Malec's lab works on causal inference and literature-based discovery; co-author Melissa L. Rethlefsen, who directs UNM's health sciences library, led the PRISMA-S search-reporting guideline. Manjil M. Pradhan is first author.

The problem

Systematic reviews sit at the top of the evidence hierarchy for chronic-disease causal inference, yet the sheer volume of literature is crushing them: a single review often takes hundreds to over a thousand person-hours and a median of 60-plus weeks from registration to publication, with screening and data extraction the two most labor-intensive steps.

Worse, the observational studies these reviews summarize are wildly inconsistent in how they adjust for confounding — omitting true confounders, or wrongly conditioning on colliders, mediators, instruments — so for the same exposure–outcome pair, different studies adjust for different variables.

The principles exist (VanderWeele's disjunctive cause criterion, DAGs), but reported practice stays all over the map. Existing ML screeners like Abstrackr are mostly proprietary black boxes, weak on transparency and reproducibility.

What's missing is a transparent, open method that both cuts the workload and makes reported exposures, outcomes, and covariates comparable across studies.

The approach

An end-to-end, two-pipeline workflow, entirely on open models (Deepseek-R1:70b, Llama3.3:70b via Ollama) and provider-agnostic.

LitScreen screens in three phases, the idea being "add evidence progressively, spend heavy compute only on the few most uncertain papers." Phase 1 is domain-neutral sentence extraction (llama3.3:70b, few-shot): every specific exposure/outcome/design name is deliberately stripped from the prompt and the few-shot examples so the model can't just keyword-match — it only pulls sentences describing design, exposures, and outcomes.

Phase 2 is criterion-wise adjudication (Deepseek-R1:70b, a reasoning model emitting <think> traces): preliminary read → self-reflection against 4–5 criterion questions → YES/NO/UNCLEAR plus a confidence level, then a deterministic three-way gate — a high-confidence NO excludes immediately, everything else escalates to full text.

Phase 3 is full-text RAG verification: Entrez resolves PMID/PMCID to JATS XML (Unpaywall as fallback), a custom parser keeps only Methods and Results, adaptive chunking (cosine>0.80, 60–500 words), bioLinkBERT-large retrieval → MMR top-50 → bge-reranker top-5 → Deepseek verifies, now barred from UNCLEAR and required to cite a chunk id.

Two anti-hallucination gates carry the design: an evidence-state gate (Disqualifying/Confirming/Insufficient, where a No is only allowed on Disqualifying) and a verbatim gate (the DISQUALIFIER_VERBATIM field must be an exact substring of the extracted sentences, or the No is demoted to Unclear). The whole thing is recall-first — dropping one eligible paper is far worse than keeping one borderline.

VarEx reuses the same parsing and chunking but keeps the abstract too (per STROBE) and pulls tables plus table/figure captions as text.

It embeds with PubMedBERT, retrieves exposure, outcome, and covariate separately with role-specific queries (up to 4 chunks per role), and prompts one role at a time; every extracted variable must carry an exact supporting quote plus chunk id, and the model is told not to generate statistical conclusions.

Covariates are then sorted into 10 semantic categories (sociodemographic, biology/genetics, health behaviors, medication, physiological measurement, psychosocial, clinical symptoms, psychiatric/medical history, underlying conditions, misc), aligned to Metaconfoundr's Confounder Matrix.

Key results

On the primary HTN→ADRD reference set (20 papers, 240 reference covariates): extraction Precision 0.80, Recall 0.79, F1 0.76, classification accuracy 0.97, strict F1 (variable and category both correct) 0.73. Education→Dementia (10 papers) reaches F1 0.81.

Across the SYNERGY validation sets, Bos is best (F1 0.85) and Brouwer (psychological predictors → depression relapse) worst (F1 0.65, classification accuracy just 0.49).

Category-level performance is very uneven: sociodemographics, physiological, and health behaviors all hit ~0.87, while Medication Use collapses to 0.57 (recall 0.47), Underlying Health Conditions is over-extracted (precision 0.60), and a sparse class like Psychosocial sits at 0.33.

On efficiency, against Wang et al.'s manual baseline (abstract 0.9, full-text 7, extraction 53 min/ref, 60.9 total) the pipeline runs 8.68 min/ref, an 85.7% saving.

But read the fine print: abstract screening is actually slower with the LLM (2.95 vs 0.9 min); nearly all the saving comes from data extraction (53→2.53). LitScreen excludes 60.8% of records on average at the abstract stage (94.1% for Education, 86.9% for HTN).

How solid is the evidence

The preprint has inconsistent signs of completion. The body still contains a "MONKEY MONKEY MONKEY TO ADD TO DO" placeholder, the HTN→ADRD PRISMA diagram has several blank n= fields, and a "Things To Do / Incomplete" block remains. Yet later pages already contain the MEDLINE strategy, screening prompt templates, and screening results in Appendix F.

Table 9 reports LitScreen screening performance. BPV→Dementia has precision 0.846 and recall 1.000; recall is 1.000 for Bos, Brouwer, van de Schoot, and Wolters, and 0.725 for van der Valk.

The HTN→ADRD and Education→Dementia production corpora lack complete inclusion labels, so expert review can report precision of 0.857 and 0.655 but cannot estimate accuracy, F1, or recall. High recall is therefore supported in several labeled benchmarks, not yet established in the production corpora.

The extraction evaluation is also generous. Semantic matching counts a true positive at cosine ≥ 0.60, a fairly loose bar that lets near-misses pass; the classification metrics are computed only on already-correctly-extracted covariates, so 0.97 is conditional on that ~79%. More awkwardly, retrieval and evaluation share the same PubMedBERT embedding family, which can turn surface agreement into apparent correctness.

The samples are small: 20 papers in the primary set, a single expert-built extraction standard, no inter-annotator agreement reported for the reference (SYNERGY had two reviewers). Brouwer is consistently the floor, showing psychological/behavioral covariate language is harder — the "generalization" that looks good is really the home turf (HTN, Education).

The efficiency comparison uses a cross-study manual baseline, not a head-to-head on the same corpus, and that 53 min/ref extraction baseline carries most of the 85.7%.

The authors concede hallucination is inevitable (variables not present in the source, decisions on the wrong evidence sentence) and position the tool as an assistant only.
