Using Causal Diagrams for Biomedical Research

Demetrios N. Kyriacou, Philip Greenland, Mohammad A. Mansournia

Annals of Emergency Medicine

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Causal DAGConfounder adjustmentCollider biasCovariate selection

30-second read

Variable roles depend on the research question and temporal order. Block every open backdoor path, avoid conditioning on colliders, and distinguish total, direct, and indirect effects when studying mediation.

Question
How can a DAG determine which variables to adjust for, which to exclude, and when a separate mediation analysis is needed?
Design
A clinical methods tutorial using Framingham Heart Study data, with diabetes–cardiac ischemia and obesity–cardiac ischemia examples for confounding, colliders, and mediation.
Evidence strength Moderate
What was assessed
DAG decision rules plus Cox models and mediation analyses involving diabetes, obesity, age, hypertension, MACE, and cardiac ischemia in Framingham data.
Downgrade triggers
The teaching DAGs are deliberately simplified and depend on prior causal knowledge. Observational estimates remain vulnerable to unmeasured and residual confounding, misclassification, and time-varying processes.
Impact on interpretation
DAGs constrain variable selection and expose bias from mechanical adjustment, but they do not validate the arrows or automatically turn observational associations into causal effects.

DAG-driven variable handling

Full deep read

A clinician-facing DAG primer: use the confounder/collider/mediator rules to decide which variables go into a multivariable model and which stay out, then let Framingham numbers show how the wrong choice miscalculates the effect.

The authors

Led by Demetrios N. Kyriacou, professor of emergency and preventive medicine at Northwestern's Feinberg School of Medicine, with Northwestern cardiovascular epidemiologist Philip Greenland and Mohammad A. Mansournia of Tehran University of Medical Sciences, who publishes widely on causal-inference methods.

The problem

Picking covariates in clinical research, many people still lean on mechanical algorithms — stepwise, significance, change-in-estimate — letting software pick the set that fits best. The trouble is these methods optimize overall fit and never ask what role each variable plays in the causal structure.

That yields two systematic errors: leaving out a confounder that belongs in, and putting in colliders or mediators that don't.

Both bias the estimate, through different mechanisms. What the authors want is for researchers, working with statisticians, to be able to say from a causal diagram why this variable is in and that one is out, instead of outsourcing variable selection to an algorithm. An old problem, but worth restating in a clinical (emergency-medicine) journal.

The approach

The core move is to treat the DAG as the basis for variable selection. A DAG is directed (arrows follow time order) and acyclic (no feedback loops); a path is any unbroken route along or against arrows, and a causal path is a run of arrows all pointing the same way. Each variable type has its own rule:

  • Confounder, three criteria: an extraneous risk factor for the outcome, independent of the exposure; associated with the exposure in the source population (directly or via a common cause); not affected by exposure or outcome, and never on the causal path between them. If it qualifies, adjust.
  • A collider is the common effect of two variables, and conditioning on it manufactures an association out of nothing (collider / selection bias), so exclude it; a mediator sits on the causal path, and adjusting it with ordinary regression erases the part of the effect that flows through it, so you need a formal mediation analysis to split direct and indirect effects.

To find a sufficient adjustment set, use backdoor paths: start from an arrow into the exposure (the backdoor), follow an open path (no colliders) to the outcome, and block every backdoor path by adjusting at least one variable on it. The authors argue for using the conventional three criteria and the backdoor method together.

Running through it all is one line: explanatory modeling is not predictive modeling, so don't judge by mechanical selection or change-in-estimate alone.

Key results

The demonstration uses Framingham, 4,287 people, exposure diabetes, outcome cardiac ischemia. Table 1 adds variables step by step: unadjusted HR 2.56; adjusting obesity alone drops it to 2.32 (9.4% change, obesity is a confounder); adding age drops it to 2.08 (another 10.3%, age is a confounder too). So far this is confounding removal.

The trap is step three: put in MACE (major adverse cardiovascular events) and the HR plunges from 2.08 to 1.28 (a 38.5% drop), the CI crossing 1. But MACE is a common effect of diabetes and cardiac ischemia — a collider, not a confounder — so that 38.5% looks like confounding removal and is actually collider bias introduced.

The same "estimate moved" event is right for obesity and age, wrong for MACE, and change-in-estimate can't tell them apart.

The mediation case is the twin: obesity on cardiac ischemia unadjusted 1.61, ordinary regression on hypertension drops it to 1.45 (9.9%), but hypertension is a mediator, so that drop erases the indirect effect; a formal mediation analysis gives total 1.69, direct 1.52, indirect 1.11 (direct × indirect ≈ total).

How solid is the evidence

It's a tutorial, not a study, so "solid" means how honest it is — and the authors are honest.

They draw the limits themselves: a DAG depends on prior scientific knowledge that is often incomplete or unavailable; whether a variable is a confounder, collider, or mediator depends on temporal order and measurement timing; a diagram that omits variables or arrows is oversimplified, and the figures here are deliberately so ("include arrows when in doubt").

They flag real problems: a variable can be a confounder and a collider at once (M-bias); the definition of confounding has been argued over for decades, with the conventional and backdoor criteria only largely overlapping; and every variable here is dichotomized, which itself introduces residual confounding. The diagram's power is only as good as the data and the prior knowledge behind it.
