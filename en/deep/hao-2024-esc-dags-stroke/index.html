Construction Specification of Directed Acyclic Graphs: An Introduction to the ESC-DAGs Method and Case Interpretation in Cerebrovascular Disease Research

郝允逸, 夏雪, 胥芹, 赵性泉 et al.

中国卒中杂志 19卷10期（2024）：1221–1229

30-second read

The case turns an English method into executable steps and suggests broader search and variable summarization. Study provenance must remain, and node merging can change adjustment sets.

Question
How do ESC-DAGs mapping, translation, and integration apply to serum uric acid and functional outcome after ischemic stroke?
Design
A Chinese methods tutorial and case study converting PubMed papers' exposures, outcomes, and controls into implied graphs, auditing edges, integrating them, and discussing the focal-search paradox and workload.
Evidence strength Methods case study
What was assessed
Exposure, outcome, controls, candidate edges, and mapping–translation–integration records from stroke uric-acid papers.
Downgrade triggers
Edges remain after temporality, face validity, theory, and potential-outcomes checks; nodes merge only when meanings and paths align, because otherwise the bias structure changes.
Impact on interpretation
The article supports training and Chinese decision logs. It does not replace primary-study bias appraisal or establish a causal uric-acid effect.

Full deep read

This Chinese case study walks through ESC-DAGs and identifies the focal-search paradox and edge-by-edge workload, helping clinical teams calibrate expectations before implementation.

The authors

郝允逸 and 赵性泉 are based at the Neurology Center of Beijing Tiantan Hospital, Capital Medical University. 夏雪, 胥芹, and 王安心 are affiliated with the Beijing Neurosurgical Institute and the Department of Clinical Epidemiology and Clinical Trials at Capital Medical University. The group works in stroke and clinical epidemiology.

The problem

Clinical researchers know that DAGs can guide confounder selection but have few Chinese, stepwise examples grounded in stroke research. The article introduces ESC-DAGs and uses baseline or admission serum uric acid and functional prognosis after ischemic stroke to show how regression papers become graphs, edges are audited, and graphs are integrated.

The approach

Mapping converts each paper's exposure, outcome, and controls into a saturated implied graph. Translation reviews temporality, face validity, theory, and a potential-outcomes thought experiment for both directions of every edge. Integration adds retained edges to a blank graph, reviews newly possible links, and may group nodes with similar inputs and outputs under a broader construct.

The stroke example searches PubMed for serum uric acid and functional outcome and shows how two studies create implied graphs. A variable adjusted in a paper is not automatically accepted as a confounder; reviewers must justify temporal order and whether setting alternative values would change the outcome.

Key results

This is a methods tutorial without a new cohort effect estimate. Figure 1 explains chains, forks, and colliders; Table 1 lists the three ESC-DAGs stages; Figures 4–6 show implied graphs, translated DAGs, and integration for the uric-acid example. Some age and sex nodes are combined into a demographic construct for readability.

The authors identify two operational problems. Searching only the focal exposure–outcome relation can miss other relations defining confounding structure. Mapping every paper and translating each edge in both directions is labor-intensive. They suggest broader searches for causes of exposure and outcome and, in complex projects, summarizing variables before joint processing.

How solid is the evidence

As a secondary methods article, it faithfully explains the original ESC-DAGs workflow and offers a locally relevant example. It expands only a few papers, performs no complete review or final effect analysis, and does not show that the proposed shortcut is equivalent to the original process. Edge decisions still depend on timing and clinical knowledge.

Combining specific nodes improves readability but can hide different time windows and pathways. Serum uric acid may reflect kidney function, metabolic status, or acute stress; an imprecise node changes the meaning of the adjustment set.
