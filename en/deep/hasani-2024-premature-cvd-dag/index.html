Constructing causal pathways for premature cardiovascular disease mortality using directed acyclic graphs with integrating evidence synthesis and expert knowledge

Wan Shakira Rodzlan Hasani, Kamarul Imran Musa, Xin Wee Chen, Kueh Yee Cheng

30-second read

Forty-five paths reach consensus and expert-preferred Model 3 has the highest AUC. Consensus and fit support feasibility but do not validate the true causal structure.

Question
How can an initial ESC-DAG become an executable premature-CVD-mortality graph through anonymous expert consensus and real-data stress testing?
Design
A three-phase case study: literature-built DAG, two-round revision by 12 experts with Fuzzy Delphi ratings of 80 paths, and comparison of three measurable DAGs in linked survey–mortality data.
Evidence strength Low to moderate
What was assessed
Expert ratings for 80 candidate paths, 45 consensus edges, three adjustment structures, and logistic fit and classification metrics.
Downgrade triggers
Edges pass with d≤0.2 and agreement ≥75%, measuring opinion concentration. Slightly higher AUC measures discrimination; neither tests causal truth of edges.
Impact on interpretation
The expert-disagreement workflow and rationales are reusable. Expert agreement or predictive accuracy cannot determine that a causal edge is true.

Evidence synthesis, Fuzzy Delphi consensus, and a real-data demonstration form a three-stage workflow; expert–model agreement supports feasibility, not validation of a true causal graph.

The authors

Wan Shakira Rodzlan Hasani, Kamarul Imran Musa, and Kueh Yee Cheng are affiliated with Universiti Sains Malaysia; Xin Wee Chen with Universiti Teknologi MARA. The team covers community medicine, public health, and biostatistics and uses linked Malaysian national health survey and mortality data.

The problem

ESC-DAGs can generate an initial graph from literature, but experts often disagree on omitted nodes and edge directions. Using premature cardiovascular mortality, the authors create a structured expert-revision and consensus rule, then examine how feasible consensus graphs behave with available survey data.

The approach

Phase 1 builds an initial ESC-DAG. Phase 2 interviews 12 public-health, epidemiology, and clinical experts separately. Eleven experts then rate 80 causal statements on a seven-point scale. The Fuzzy Delphi object is each candidate path. A threshold d≤0.2 and agreement ≥75% trigger retention; the practical effect is keeping, removing, or renaming an edge.

Phase 3 proposes three data-feasible DAGs for total effects of diabetes, hypertension, and hyperlipidemia on premature cardiovascular mortality. Experts rate validity, feasibility, and adjustment sets. Logistic regression compares AIC, AUC, pseudo-R², and classification metrics.

Key results

Of 80 second-round statements, 35 paths fail the joint agreement and d thresholds, leaving 45 consensus paths. Experts rank Model 1 as most feasible. Model 3 ranks first for structural validity and adjustment-set agreement, with 91.67% agreement on validity.

Model 3 has the lowest AIC (3,903.22) and highest AUC (82.02%), versus 81.46% for Model 1. Model 1 has slightly higher accuracy, 76.31% versus 75.95%. Diagnosed diabetes has an OR of about 3.30–3.53 across the three adjustments.

How solid is the evidence

The study quantifies disagreement and records edge-removal reasons, offering more transparency than an informal panel. The panel is small, with 11 second-round respondents. Agreement and d measure concentration of opinion, not truth, and shared context may limit external validity.

AUC, AIC, and accuracy are predictive or fit metrics. Model 3's AUC advantage is only 0.46–0.56 points and its accuracy is slightly lower; none validates causal structure. Key survey variables are self-reported, and simplifying the graph to measured nodes can leave residual confounding and overfit the setting.
