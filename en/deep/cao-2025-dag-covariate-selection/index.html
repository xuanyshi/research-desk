Directed acyclic graphs in choosing covariates for multivariable model of observational study

曹雨滋, 袁驰, 刘刚, 孙凤 et al.

华西医学 40卷6期（2025）

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Causal DAGCovariate selectionMultivariable analysisConfoundingCollider biasObservational research

30-second read

Causal structure determines covariate roles; statistical association only supplements prior knowledge. DAGs improve transparency but do not quantify confounding or solve unmeasured confounding.

Question
Why can P-value or change-in-estimate selection fail in observational models, and how do DAGs avoid adjusting mediators, instruments, and colliders?
Design
A Chinese narrative methods review comparing four data-driven procedures, DAG path rules, and causal selection criteria, illustrated with a type 2 diabetes–fracture example.
Evidence strength Methods review
What was assessed
Candidate covariate positions in an exposure–outcome DAG, open and closed paths, and adjustment sets from different selection rules.
Downgrade triggers
An open backdoor requires blocking by a non-descendant of exposure; conditioning on a mediator or collider can block the target effect or open a new biasing path.
Impact on interpretation
The graph identifies primary adjustment and prohibited variables. Unmeasured confounding, measurement error, and alternative DAGs still require sensitivity analysis.

Full deep read

This Chinese review places data-driven covariate-selection errors, DAG path rules, and clinical adjustment decisions in one framework, making it a useful applied introduction.

The authors

曹雨滋, 袁驰, and 李舍予 are affiliated with Endocrinology and the MAGIC China Center at West China Hospital, Sichuan University; 刘刚 with Tongji Medical College, Huazhong University of Science and Technology; 孙凤 with Epidemiology and Biostatistics at Peking University. The team spans clinical endocrinology, evidence-based medicine, and epidemiology.

The problem

Observational studies often select covariates using stepwise regression, univariable P values, or change-in-estimate rules. These association-driven procedures can miss common causes or condition on mediators, instruments, and colliders. The review explains how DAGs separate a variable's causal role from statistical association.

The approach

The article distinguishes confounders, effect modifiers, mediators, instruments, colliders, and descendants, then compares forward selection, backward elimination, the 10% change-in-estimate rule, and high-dimensional propensity scores. A type 2 diabetes–fracture example shows why statistical significance or estimate change does not establish adjustment validity.

Graph development follows ESC-DAGs: map study claims, review edges through temporality, face validity, theory, and counterfactual reasoning, then integrate. The adjustment object is every open noncausal exposure–outcome path. The backdoor criterion triggers blocking of all biasing paths; the practical effect is a minimal sufficient set excluding mediators, colliders, and unnecessary instruments.

Key results

This is a narrative methods review without new effect estimates. It summarizes four data-driven procedures, DAG path-opening rules, and alternatives including common-cause, pretreatment-variable, and disjunctive-cause criteria. DAGitty can calculate minimal sufficient sets and test whether a proposed set blocks all biasing paths.

One example reports an adjusted OR of 0.80 after a statistically selected variable is included, yet that variable may be a collider; estimate change alone is not evidence for adjustment. The review also notes that roughly two-thirds of applied DAG studies explicitly displayed their graph, with fewer reporting construction assumptions systematically.

How solid is the evidence

The article clearly synthesizes classic DAG logic and recent applied recommendations for a Chinese clinical audience. It is not a systematic review and provides no simulation or empirical comparison of selection strategies. Numerical examples and prevalence claims still depend on the cited sources.

A DAG determines adjustment under an assumed structure but does not quantify confounding. An incomplete graph yields a wrong set, and multiple valid sets cannot identify the correct graph from data alone. Measurement error and unmeasured confounding require separate sensitivity analyses.
