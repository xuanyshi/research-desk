Causal diagramming for assessing human system risk in spaceflight

Erik Antonsen, Robert J. Reynolds, Jacqueline Charvat, Mary Van Baalen et al.

npj Microgravity

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Causal DAGExpert elicitationSpaceflight medicineEvidence grading

30-second read

DAGs have become tools for NASA risk communication and configuration management and have produced a first cross-risk network. Current evidence mainly supports process feasibility; quantitative risk analysis and decision utility remain future work.

Question
Can NASA use standardized DAGs to integrate roughly 30 interdependent astronaut health risks and turn expert consensus into an evolving risk network?
Design
A pilot-process and methods review. Since 2020, the HSRB has built Narrative and Detailed DAGs for each risk, harmonized nodes, edges, terms, and evidence styles, and demonstrated the approach with SANS, bone remodeling, and a combined risk network.
Evidence strength Preliminary
What was assessed
About 30 risks, five hazards, eight DRMs, a 100–200-member review forum, successive SANS graphs, and structural checks using four rodent datasets.
Downgrade triggers
Most edges rely on SMEs and a thin spaceflight evidence base. Animal data test only part of one bone-fracture DAG; the combined network is not an official HSRB product and no comparative decision outcomes are reported.
Impact on interpretation
The work supports collaboration, gap annotation, and version tracking. It does not establish causal discovery, individual risk prediction, or intervention simulation.

Full deep read

NASA manages roughly 30 astronaut-health risks with DAGs: experts build the graphs by hand, grade each edge by level of evidence, run them through configuration review, and stitch them into a risk network.

The authors

First author Erik Antonsen chairs NASA's Human System Risk Board and is at Baylor College of Medicine; senior author Daniel M. Buckland is at NASA Johnson Space Center and Duke University. The team applies causal diagrams (DAGs) to astronaut health risks.

The problem

NASA tracks about 30 risks to astronaut health and performance, all deriving from the 5 hazards of spaceflight, and evaluates each against 8 design reference missions (from short low-Earth-orbit trips to three-year Mars missions).

These risks were historically characterized in separate silos, which worked while the risk records were being built, but astronauts are exposed to every risk at once, and the risk itself is multifactorial and cumulative.

Siloing left different disciplines talking past each other: altered gravity alone went by six names (microgravity, weightlessness, partial gravity, and so on). With no common terminology and no shared mental model, the Human System Risk Board (HSRB) struggled to agree on which risk is more urgent and where to spend limited resources.

The evidence base makes it worse: fewer than 700 humans have ever flown, so many causal links rest on expert judgment.

What they needed was not another schematic but a representation that makes complex causality explicit and can be tracked and managed by the organization.

The approach

From January 2020 the HSRB drew each risk as a DAG: nodes are random variables, directed edges denote causality, no cycles. Every graph starts at a hazard and ends at a predefined mission-level outcome (from loss of mission objectives to loss of crew life). Each risk gets two kinds of graph.

A narrative DAG tells the high-level causal story with an accompanying written walkthrough, used to align mental models at board meetings; a detailed DAG fills in specifics but must obey the same data schema. Acyclicity is a hard constraint: if a feedback loop appears, the creators pick the most likely predecessor node and place it earlier in the chain.

Getting a node into a graph has thresholds. First there must be a logical basis that the node affects the risk; the HSRB adopted 6 of Bradford Hill's criteria to grade level of evidence, with temporality and analogy as the minimum needed to reach the lowest "speculative" level and enter the DAG. For a narrative DAG the effect must also be large enough to move a downstream node measurably.

The representation is standardized almost obsessively: nodes are circles, edges run from cause to effect, exogenous is distinguished from endogenous; countermeasures split into monitoring / prevention / intervention (the carbon-monoxide case: detector → CO level → oxygen masks); category nodes can nest sub-nodes (individual factors houses modifiable / non-modifiable).

Terminology is harmonized and named as neutral random variables — nutritional status, not inadequate nutrition — so a node is reusable across risks and across DRMs; overlapping nodes across risks are aligned by "edge work," which also guards against accidental cycles.

Governance is the heavy part.

Each risk is drafted by a risk custodian team, reviewed by the HSRB as a forum of 100 to 200 people, run through a formal tracked-comment and comment-resolution process, with disagreements settled by the board or its chair against evidence standards; only the narrative DAG is configuration-managed, the detailed one is not (too much detail, too much time).

They also piloted structural validation: a subset of the bone-fracture DAG against 4 rodent datasets, testing the conditional independencies the DAG implies, and the data largely supported the structure.

Level of evidence is drawn straight into the edge style: solid for strong, dotted for weak (the SANS DAG was approved April 2020, revised May 2022).

Finally the 30 detailed DAGs were joined through common nodes into a single risk network, which the authors admit is "beyond manual approaches" to analyze; the next step is to run it as a Bayesian network with epistemic and aleatory uncertainty built in, but that is future work.

Key results

A methods/process piece, no effect sizes — the output is a reusable graph-building standard plus a governance mechanism that actually runs.

The concrete artifacts: about 30 configuration-managed risks, 5 hazards, 8 DRMs; two DAG types per risk; 6 Hill-derived evidence criteria, with temporality + analogy as the entry floor and "speculative" as the lowest grade; three countermeasure categories; tooling moved from Dagitty to the in-house DAGvision; a 100–200-person review forum; one structural validation on 4 rodent datasets; a first risk network stitched from all 30 graphs.

Evidence grading isn't just talk — it is encoded in edge style and updated across versions (the SANS DAG's two-year revision is the example).

How solid is the evidence

What's validated here is not a hypothesis but the claim that the process is workable, so the bar sits at feasibility. The causal structure comes mostly from expert judgment, not learned from data; the authors concede the evidence base is thin and many edges are speculative.

Several real problems are named but unsolved: how to grade evidence objectively (the 6 Hill criteria are still a judgment call, and someone has to set the level boundaries), the anchoring bias the first DAG imposes, and the lack of consensus on naming and granularity, which in the end is settled by the HSRB's administrative authority declaring the "official" DAG.

The defense of acyclicity — feedback loops are never simultaneous and can be shown with time-indexed nodes — is internally coherent, but they admit that collapsing duration of exposure into a single node is a stopgap.

The 30-risk network reads more like an existence proof: it can be assembled, but quantitative analysis is explicitly future work. As engineering practice it is solid; as a causal-inference method it still stops at structured expert consensus.
