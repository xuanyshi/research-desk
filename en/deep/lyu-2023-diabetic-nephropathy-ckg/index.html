Causal knowledge graph construction and evaluation for clinical decision support of diabetic nephropathy

Kewei Lyu, Yu Tian, Yong Shang, Tianshu Zhou et al.

Journal of Biomedical Informatics 139 (2023):104298
DiabetesKidney disease

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Causal knowledge graphDiabetic nephropathyClinical decision supportElectronic health recordsGraph pruningKnowledge evaluation

30-second read

The graph covers most diagnostic evidence and pruning raises expert accuracy by 5.5 points. This supports knowledge-engineering and decision-support use, not counterfactual validity of triples.

Question
Can multi-source medical causal statements form a graph for differential diagnosis of diabetic nephropathy, with patient data and expert review revealing noise?
Design
An engineering evaluation combining SemMedDB, UpToDate, and a diagnostic textbook, mapped to about 1,400 inpatients and evaluated by three graph models, clinical text, and expert review.
Evidence strength Moderate
What was assessed
Patient graphs for 1,427 people, 150 diagnostic narratives, and sampled expert-rated triples, assessed by AUC, F1, path coverage, and manual accuracy.
Downgrade triggers
SemMedDB supplies 99.1% of edges and the EHR cohort is single-center; systematic extraction, publication, or threshold bias could overstate transferability of predictive gains.
Impact on interpretation
The multi-source, provenance, and pruning workflow is reusable. AUC, coverage, and expert plausibility cannot be read directly as causal-effect evidence.

From multi-source knowledge to patient-level evaluation

Full deep read

This study links causal statements from literature, guidelines, and textbooks to patient-level graph reasoning; it demonstrates engineering utility, not counterfactual validity of the edges.

The authors

Kewei Lyu, Yu Tian, and corresponding author Jingsong Li are affiliated with Zhejiang University and the Research Center for Healthcare Data Science at Zhejiang Lab. Xi Yao, Ping Zhang, and Jianghua Chen are based at the Kidney Disease Center of the First Affiliated Hospital, Zhejiang University. The collaboration spans knowledge engineering, electronic records, and nephrology.

The problem

Medical causal knowledge graphs are often justified by extraction scale. Two practical questions receive less attention: whether a graph covers diagnostic evidence in real records, and whether noisy edges degrade clinical decision support. The authors use differential diagnosis of diabetic versus non-diabetic kidney disease to assess completeness, predictive use, and accuracy after pruning.

The approach

The graph combines SemMedDB, UpToDate, and Churchill's Pocketbook of Differential Diagnosis. Causes, produces, and predisposes relations are normalized, with PMID or source provenance retained. Structured, semi-structured, and narrative EHR evidence is mapped into patient-specific graphs. Two pruning rules remove SemMedDB edges using literature co-occurrence and causal-relation ratios.

Evaluation uses 1,427 inpatients and 3,045 admissions from one Chinese hospital between October 2010 and October 2020. Patient-level training, validation, and test sets contain 856, 286, and 285 people. R-GCN, MHGRN, and MedPath compare knowledge sources, causal versus associative relations, and pruning thresholds; 150 patient narratives and expert samples assess coverage and accuracy.

Key results

The graph contains 153,289 concepts and 1,719,968 causal triples, 99.1% from SemMedDB. Mapping was nearly 100% for structured data and 93.5% for semi-structured data. Among 1,783 diagnostic evidence items in 150 patients, causal paths covered 93.6%; the abstract reports overall causal-relation coverage of 93.9%.

Combining all three sources improved AUC and F1 over SemMedDB alone for all three models (P<0.01). Models using only the three causal relations did not differ significantly from models using all nine relations, whereas models excluding them performed worse. Pruning improved AUC and F1 (P<0.01) and raised expert-rated triple accuracy by 5.5 percentage points.

How solid is the evidence

Real EHRs, model ablations, and expert review make this stronger than a graph-size demonstration. The outcomes remain diagnostic prediction and text coverage. An edge helping a model, or appearing plausible to an expert, does not show that intervening on its cause would change the outcome. The graph also inherits extraction and publication biases from SemMedDB, which supplies 99.1% of its triples.

The cohort is single-center and dominated by older men. Threshold selection in the same application can overfit pruning to that task. Edges are not graded by study design, target population, estimand, or risk of bias.
