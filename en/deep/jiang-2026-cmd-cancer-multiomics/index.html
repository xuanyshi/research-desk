Multiomics insight into disease trajectories of cardiometabolic diseases and cancer

Xuanwei Jiang, Guangrui Yang, Meng Chen, Nannan Feng et al.

Nature Communications 17:813, 2026CAS 1 TopJCR Q1IF 18.1
Cancer

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Multistate modelDisease trajectoriesMultimorbidityMultiomicsProteomicsUK BiobankRisk predictionCancer epidemiology

30-second read

About 15,100 participants develop CMD–cancer multimorbidity and lose about five survival years versus healthy participants. Prognostic differences by disease order vary with cancer survival and CMD severity, while proteomic scores add more discrimination than genomic or metabolomic scores.

Question
How do cardiometabolic disease and cancer form multimorbidity over time, does disease order affect prognosis, and can multiomics predict nine transitions?
Design
A UK Biobank multistate cohort and multiomics prediction study of about 430,000 participants, five states and nine transitions, with genomic, metabolomic, and proteomic scores and external pattern validation in HRS.
Evidence strength Moderate
What was assessed
Nine transitions in about 430,000 participants, restricted mean survival, propensity-score matching, three omics scores, replication splits, and transition-pattern validation in about 8,400 HRS participants.
Downgrade triggers
The multistate model describes observational transitions and prediction, not intervention effects. UK Biobank has healthy-volunteer and ancestry limitations, proteomics includes only about 44,800 people, and prediction lacks independent external validation.
Impact on interpretation
The study is a useful natural-history and risk-stratification map, but disease order and omics markers should not be read directly as causal pathways or deployment-ready tools.

CMD–cancer multistate and multiomics analysis

04

Full deep read

Tracking how cardiometabolic disease and cancer stack into multimorbidity and then death across 429,000 UK Biobank participants: multimorbidity costs about five years of life, which disease comes first shifts mortality risk, and proteomics reads the trajectory earlier than genomics.

The authors

Victor W. Zhong's group in the Department of Epidemiology and Biostatistics, School of Public Health, Shanghai Jiao Tong University School of Medicine, with Xuanwei Jiang and Guangrui Yang as co–first authors and co-corresponding roles for Xihao Du (SJTU) and Chunlai Zeng (Lishui).

Zhong, previously at Northwestern, works on cardiometabolic and nutritional epidemiology and has a run of UK Biobank multimorbidity studies; this continues that line. Published in Nature Communications.

The problem

Multimorbidity is a defining problem of aging populations: roughly a third of people aged 45–65 live with two or more chronic diseases, over 80% among those 80+. Yet most research manages one disease at a time, leaving no map of how multiple chronic diseases evolve into and off each other over time.

Cardiometabolic disease (CMD — cardiovascular disease plus type 2 diabetes) and cancer are two leading causes of death; they're known to co-occur, share risk factors like aging and obesity, and share pathways like inflammation and hypoxia — but who comes first, how much higher the mortality of multimorbidity is over single disease, and whether temporal order changes outcomes were unresolved.

A second gap is prediction: single-disease omics prediction already reaches 10–15 years ahead, but for high-multimorbidity-risk elders, predicting one disease isn't enough, and whether multiomics can capture the transitions between diseases was untested.

The approach

They cast each person's course as a multistate model: five states — healthy, CMD only, cancer only, CMD-cancer multimorbidity, death — linked by nine transitions (e.g., healthy→CMD, CMD→cancer, CMD→cancer→death, cancer→CMD→death).

Across 429,555 UK Biobank participants over a median 14.5 years, they estimated transition probabilities, mean survival times, and mortality risk, deliberately separating "CMD before cancer" from "cancer before CMD." To make states comparable, they used propensity score matching to balance baseline.

The second part is multiomics: in genomics (375,239), metabolomics (234,452), and proteomics (44,816) datasets, they built omics scores (GenScore/MetScore/ProtScore) with LASSO in a discovery set, validated in replication, and measured how much C-statistic each adds over a base model (demographics etc.) for every transition.

Subgroups covered CMD subtype (stroke, heart failure, coronary disease, T2D), cancer survival tier, benign vs. malignant, and CMD severity.

Key results

Over follow-up, 105,903 progressed to single morbidity and 15,088 to CMD-cancer multimorbidity. Multimorbidity carried a 28–33% higher death probability than healthy (5.0–5.3 fewer years of life) and 11–23% higher than single morbidity (2.4–3.6 fewer years); progression to multimorbidity rose sharply about 5 years after either first diagnosis.

Mean survival times: healthy 14.8 years, CMD only 13.2, cancer only 12.3, multimorbidity ~9.5–9.9.

Order matters but flips by cancer type: overall, CMD-first was 1–2% more lethal than cancer-first (0.3 more years lost), but for high-survival cancers CMD-first was 7% lower, for low-survival cancers 9% higher, and 3% higher in the malignant subgroup.

Cancer type dominated — pancreatic cancer had 72% direct mortality; 25% of liver-cancer patients further developed CMD. Among omics, proteomics scores predicted best, with ΔC-statistic 0.03–0.14 across the nine transitions, clearly beating genomics and metabolomics scores (modest at best).

How solid is the evidence

The strengths are scale and design: 429,555 people, a median 14.5 years of follow-up, and a multistate framework that puts progression, order, and death into one coherent structure, with propensity matching improving comparability and the proteomics gain validated in replication. But several things need care.

A multistate model yields transition probabilities and associations, not intervention effects — "CMD-first is more lethal" is an observed temporal association still open to shared causes, detection timing, and competing risk.

Propensity matching only balances measured baseline; aging, obesity, and inflammation, which drive both disease families, are hard to balance, so unmeasured confounding persists. The 44,816 with proteomics are the Olink subset of the cohort, so representativeness and external validity are open questions the authors themselves flag.

The ΔC-statistic of 0.03–0.14 is real but small, and proteomics' "win" is mostly relative to genomics/metabolomics — absolute discrimination is short of clinical use.

UK Biobank's healthy-volunteer bias and largely white sample limit extrapolation.
