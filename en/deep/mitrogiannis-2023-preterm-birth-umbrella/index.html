Risk factors for preterm birth: an umbrella review of meta-analyses of observational studies

Ioannis Mitrogiannis, Evangelos Evangelou, Athina Efthymiou, Theofilos Kanavos et al.

BMC Medicine 21 (2023):494
Pregnancy

30-second read

Only seven associations meet robust criteria, all from meta-analyses with fewer than ten studies. The result is epidemiological credibility of observational associations, not causal confirmation.

Question
Among many preterm-birth meta-analyses, which associations jointly pass checks for sample size, prediction intervals, heterogeneity, and publication-bias signals?
Design
An umbrella review reanalyzing 166 associations from 85 meta-analyses and about 1,500 primary studies, graded as robust, highly suggestive, suggestive, or weak.
Evidence strength Moderate
What was assessed
Pooled effects, case counts, 95% prediction intervals, I², small-study effects, and excess significance for 166 observational associations.
Downgrade triggers
The top tier requires >1,000 cases, P<10⁻⁶, I²<50%, a non-null prediction interval, and no two bias signals; however, bias tests have low power below ten studies.
Impact on interpretation
The tier prioritizes further validation. It does not remove unmeasured confounding, reverse causation, selection bias, or exposure misclassification.

Umbrella grading of preterm-birth associations

04

Full deep read

Only seven of 166 preterm-birth factors meet prespecified epidemiological credibility criteria; the grade filters noise but remains distinct from causal evidence.

The authors

Ioannis Mitrogiannis, Stefania Papatheodorou, and colleagues bring together obstetrics, epidemiology, and evidence synthesis. The review was registered in PROSPERO (CRD42021227296) and re-analyzed observational meta-analyses under one protocol.

The problem

The literature on preterm-birth risk factors is large, while significant findings are vulnerable to heterogeneity, small-study effects, and selective reporting. The authors seek a broad map and identify associations meeting joint requirements for sample size, statistical strength, prediction intervals, and bias diagnostics.

The approach

PubMed and Scopus were searched through February 2021 for systematic reviews and meta-analyses of observational studies. For overlapping reviews of the same factor, the most comprehensive source was retained. Each association was re-estimated with random effects, 95% CI, 95% prediction interval, I², small-study effects, and excess significance.

The object graded as robust evidence is one risk-factor meta-analysis. It is triggered by more than 1,000 cases, P<10⁻⁶, I²<50%, a prediction interval excluding the null, and no small-study or excess-significance signal. The practical effect is placement in the highest epidemiological credibility tier. The rule does not test unmeasured confounding or whether the exposure is intervenable.

Key results

The review analyzes 85 meta-analyses, 1,480 primary studies, and 166 associations. Ninety-nine associations are significant at P<0.05 and 41 at P<10⁻⁶. Ninety-one have large or very large heterogeneity; 37 show small-study effects and 12 show excess significance.

Seven meet robust criteria: amphetamine exposure, isolated single umbilical artery, maternal personality disorder, objectively assessed sleep-disordered breathing, prior induced termination with vacuum aspiration, low gestational weight gain, and an interpregnancy interval under six months after miscarriage. Twenty-six are highly suggestive and 15 suggestive.

How solid is the evidence

Standardized re-analysis and prespecified tiers improve comparability, and prediction intervals are stricter than pooled P values alone. All seven robust findings arise from meta-analyses with fewer than ten studies, where bias tests have limited power. The authors did not reassess each primary study, and omissions from source reviews carry forward.

“Robust” describes statistical and publication patterns. It does not remove confounding, reverse causation, selection bias, or exposure misclassification. Some factors are compound social or clinical states rather than direct interventions.
