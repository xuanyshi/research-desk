CLADDER: Assessing Causal Reasoning in Language Models

Zhijing Jin, Yuen Chen, Felix Leeb, Luigi Gresele et al.

Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems 36 (NeurIPS 2023)

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Large language modelsCausal reasoningCLADDERCounterfactualsBenchmarkingChain of thought

30-second read

GPT-4 rises from 62.03% to 70.40%, with higher rungs still harder. Models reconstruct graphs well but fail at query or estimand recognition and formal inference.

Question
Can LLMs convert natural-language causal questions into correct associational, interventional, and counterfactual inference rather than rely on commonsense patterns?
Design
A synthetic benchmark using graphs and an inference engine to generate about 10,000 questions with ground truth, comparing models and six-step CausalCoT across commonsense, nonsensical, and anti-commonsense contexts.
Evidence strength Moderate
What was assessed
Final answers and six intermediate reasoning stages for 10,112 closed-world questions, with truth generated by a symbolic causal engine.
Downgrade triggers
Commonsense shortcuts should fail in anti-commonsense contexts; CausalCoT improves across contexts but still misses nearly 30%, while real graph construction and unmeasured confounding are excluded.
Impact on interpretation
The benchmark supports formal-reasoning tests and agent unit tests. Synthetic accuracy is not equivalent to causal-modeling ability in real medical studies.

Full deep read

CLADDER turns natural-language questions into tasks with answers determined by a causal inference engine; GPT-4 reaches only 70.40% even with a bespoke reasoning chain.

The authors

Zhijing Jin, Yuen Chen, Felix Leeb, Luigi Gresele, and colleagues are affiliated with the Max Planck Institute for Intelligent Systems, ETH Zürich, IIT Kharagpur, the University of Hong Kong, and the University of Washington. The team combines NLP, causal inference, and cognitive science, with supervision from Bernhard Schölkopf and Mrinmaya Sachan.

The problem

Claims that a model “understands causality” often rely on commonsense questions or causal-phrase extraction, which can be solved by language patterns. CLADDER tests whether models parse a graph, classify a query, invoke the right identification rule, and calculate an answer while limiting contamination and commonsense shortcuts.

The approach

Binary and categorical causal graphs generate questions across association, intervention, and counterfactual rungs. An inference engine derives symbolic questions and ground-truth probabilities before templates verbalize them. Contexts are commonsensical, nonsensical, or anti-commonsensical.

CausalCoT uses six stages: parse the graph, identify the query, formalize it, collect data, perform causal inference, and calculate the binary answer. Evaluation covers final accuracy and intermediate node, edge, query or estimand, inference, and arithmetic performance.

Key results

CLADDER v1.5 contains 10,112 samples: 3,160, 3,160, and 3,792 across rungs 1, 2, and 3. Random accuracy is 49.27%; LLaMa scores 44.03%, Alpaca 44.66%, GPT-3.5 52.18%, and GPT-4 62.03%.

CausalCoT raises GPT-4 to 70.40%, an 8.37-point gain. Rung accuracies are 83.35%, 67.47%, and 62.05%. Anti-commonsense accuracy rises from 60.47% to 70.12%. Node and edge F1 are 99.34% and 97.01%, yet query or estimand F1 is 53% and formal-inference F1 is 50.65%.

How solid is the evidence

Ground truth comes from explicit models and an inference engine. Nonsensical and anti-commonsense contexts reduce memorization shortcuts, and stepwise evaluation localizes failures. These remain closed-world synthetic tasks: the graph, variables, and identifiability are supplied. Target populations, temporal ordering, unmeasured confounding, and measurement mechanisms are outside the benchmark.

The models and APIs are a 2023 snapshot and do not determine current-model performance. Some fine-grained evaluations use only 2,000 questions because of rate limits, and accuracy treats all errors as equally costly.
