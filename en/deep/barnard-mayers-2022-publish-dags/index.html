A case study and proposal for publishing directed acyclic graphs: The effectiveness of the quadrivalent human papillomavirus vaccine in perinatally HIV infected girls

Ruby Barnard-Mayers, Hiba Kouser, Jamie A. Cohen, Katherine Tassiopoulos et al.

30-second read

The conceptual graph gives five adjustment variables, while the data graph reveals three residual-bias sources. The paper demonstrates design and publication, not a vaccine-effect estimate.

Question
Before analyzing quadrivalent HPV vaccination in girls with perinatal HIV, how can a DAG expose confounding, misclassification, missing variables, and target-population choice?
Design
A pre-analysis case study building a vaccine–abnormal cytology conceptual DAG from literature and experts, then adapting it to PHACS data with true constructs, proxy measurements, and unavailable variables.
Evidence strength Methods case study
What was assessed
Vaccine initiation or doses, abnormal cytology, common causes, mediators, contraindications, and measurement or missingness in PHACS data.
Downgrade triggers
Mixed evidence defaults to an edge because omission implies a sharp null; a weak proxy or missing key variable triggers residual-bias and quantitative-bias analysis.
Impact on interpretation
The case supports preregistering graphs, adjustment sets, and sensitivity analyses. It does not establish HPV vaccine effects among girls with HIV.

Reading adviceUse Figures 1–2 to distinguish the skeleton from the conceptual graph, Figures 3–4 to compare ideal adjustment with data reality, then Conclusions to identify which paths available data cannot close.

From an HPV conceptual DAG to a data DAG

04

Full deep read

This case study converts a conceptual DAG into a data-specific DAG, exposing residual bias from sexual-history misclassification, proxies for structural racism, and missing maternal history.

The authors

Ruby Barnard-Mayers, Eleanor J Murray, and colleagues are affiliated with Boston University, Harvard, NYU, UCLA, the University of Miami, and the PHACS network. The team spans epidemiology, pediatric HIV, HPV, and health decision science. The DAG is published before analysis with an invitation for community feedback.

The problem

Girls with perinatally acquired HIV appeared to have more abnormal cervical cytology after quadrivalent HPV vaccination. This could reflect immune response or age, vaccination access, sexual behavior, structural factors, and measurement error. The authors aim to specify mechanisms, target population, and data limitations before analysis.

The approach

Two questions concern vaccination versus no vaccination and dose response. Exposure is split into initiation and continuation, with vaccine response as a mediator. Literature and expert discussion add nodes affecting at least two variables. Mixed or limited evidence defaults to retaining an arrow because an omitted edge encodes the stronger sharp-null assumption.

The conceptual DAG is then adapted to PHACS AMP and AMP Up data. Measured sexual history, measured antibody response, race or ethnicity as a proxy, and unavailable maternal cervical history are represented separately. Handling the yeast-allergy contraindication changes when the target population shifts from all girls with perinatal HIV to those eligible for vaccination.

Key results

This is a design case study and estimates no vaccine effect. For the total effect of vaccine initiation on abnormal cytology, the conceptual graph gives an adjustment set of age, insurance, structural racism, maternal history, and sexual history.

After adapting to data, true sexual history can remain a confounder: 14%–39% of children and adolescents with perinatal HIV exposure in an earlier study inconsistently reported lifetime partners or condom use. Structural racism and maternal cervical history are unavailable, requiring imperfect proxies or quantitative bias analysis. Antibody titers are also an imperfect measure of immune function.

How solid is the evidence

Textual rationales accompany key edges, while omitted edges are treated as assumptions requiring defense. Separating measurements from constructs links data limitations to specific residual confounding and measurement bias. Because no effect analysis is performed, the paper does not answer whether the vaccine works or whether the proposed set is sufficient in the actual data.

Some decisions rely on team knowledge, such as treating CD4 count, viral load, and ART as modifiers rather than confounders. Publishing those disagreements makes the DAG explicitly revisable.
