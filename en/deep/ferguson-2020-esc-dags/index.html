Evidence synthesis for constructing directed acyclic graphs (ESC-DAGs): a novel and systematic method for building directed acyclic graphs

Karl D Ferguson, Mark McCann, Srinivasa Vittal Katikireddi, Hilary Thomson et al.

International Journal of Epidemiology 49 (2020):322–329

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ ESC-DAGsCausal DAGEvidence synthesisCounterfactualsDecision logSensitivity analysis

30-second read

ESC-DAGs supplies mapping, translation, integration, and a decision log. It systematizes structural synthesis but does not formally weight edges by study bias or certainty.

Question
How can conclusions from observational studies become an auditable DAG for adjustment and structural sensitivity analysis?
Design
The original method maps each study to a saturated implied graph, reviews every edge using temporality, face validity, theory, and counterfactual thought experiments, then integrates retained edges.
Evidence strength Method proposal
What was assessed
Each study's exposure, outcome, controls, candidate edges, and edge-level retain, reverse, or delete decisions.
Downgrade triggers
An edge is retained after temporality and face-validity checks and remains plausible under theory and a counterfactual experiment; an edge retained from one study can enter the integrated graph.
Impact on interpretation
The workflow creates transparent candidate structures and guides adjustment or sensitivity analysis. It does not guarantee a true DAG or equal evidence strength across edges.

Structural synthesis in ESC-DAGs

04

Full deep read

ESC-DAGs maps study conclusions into saturated graphs, audits candidate edges, and integrates them; it makes graph construction traceable while leaving edge-level evidence quality unresolved.

The authors

Karl D Ferguson, Mark McCann, Srinivasa Vittal Katikireddi, Hilary Thomson, Michael J Green, and James D Lewsey are mainly affiliated with public health, mental health, and health economics groups at the University of Glasgow. Published in the International Journal of Epidemiology, this is the original ESC-DAGs methods paper.

The problem

DAGs can identify adjustment strategies through the backdoor criterion, yet papers seldom explain where their graph came from. Intuitive graph drawing is difficult to audit and can be shaped by available data or results. The authors embed evidence synthesis in the review phase and preserve decisions about nodes, edges, and deletions.

The approach

The workflow has mapping, translation, and integration. Mapping turns each study's exposure, outcome, and controls into an implied graph: exposure points to outcome, controls initially become common causes, and nodes are saturated with possible links. This represents the study's claims without judging correctness.

Translation checks both directions of each candidate edge. Failed temporality deletes or reverses an edge; face validity removes obvious implausibility; theory enters a log but its absence is not proof of no effect; a potential-outcomes thought experiment asks whether setting everyone to alternative exposure values would change the average outcome. Integration adds retained edges to a blank graph, reviews newly possible relations, and may recombine theoretically similar nodes with matching inputs and outputs.

Key results

This is a methods demonstration without effect estimates or sample-size results. A hypothetical and an empirical study of parental and adolescent alcohol use illustrate the process. An initial graph treats adolescent sex and substance use as confounders. Translation shows sex as a separate risk path and substance use as a possible mediator; indiscriminate adjustment can block the effect and open collider bias.

The integrated DAG can be analyzed in DAGitty and low-confidence or bidirectional candidate edges can define structural sensitivity analyses. The paper recommends publishing decision logs and graph code.

How solid is the evidence

The contribution is transparency, systematization, and modularity, not empirical validation. Saturated mapping provisionally treats every adjusted variable as a common cause, then relies heavily on reviewers to correct it. A biased regression conclusion is still inherited at the mapping stage.

Temporality, face validity, theory, and counterfactual questions do not become reproducible evidence weights. An edge retained from any study can enter the integrated graph without formal weighting by design, risk of bias, or independence. True structures may still be missed, and the workload is substantial.
