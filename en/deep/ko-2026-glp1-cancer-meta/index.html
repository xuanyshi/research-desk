Risk for Cancer With Glucagon-Like Peptide-1 Receptor Agonists and Dual Agonists: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis

Albert Ko, Yu-Cheng Chang, Furkan Bahar, Tsu Hsien Wang et al.

Annals of Internal Medicine 179(2):216–229, 2026CAS 1 TopJCR Q1IF 17.2
Cancer

30-second read

Moderate-certainty evidence suggests little or no effect on thyroid, pancreatic, breast, or kidney cancer; most other sites have low or very-low certainty. Median follow-up is only 70 weeks, leaving long-term harm and benefit unresolved.

Question
Do placebo-controlled RCTs support increased or decreased obesity-related cancer risk with GLP-1 receptor agonists or dual agonists?
Design
A systematic review and random-effects meta-analysis of 48 placebo-controlled RCTs and about 94,200 participants, assessing 13 cancer outcomes with RoB 2 and GRADE.
Evidence strength Higher
What was assessed
Cancer events at 13 sites, site-specific ORs, absolute risk differences, RoB 2, GRADE, and prespecified subgroup and sensitivity analyses across 48 randomized trials.
Downgrade triggers
The source trials were not designed for cancer, events were sparse, and follow-up was short. Individual data were unavailable for detailed baseline cancer-risk checks or uniform outcome adjudication.
Impact on interpretation
Randomized evidence does not support a clear short-term carcinogenic or preventive effect; interpretation should remain limited to the observed follow-up window.

Randomized cancer evidence for GLP-1 therapies

04

Full deep read

Pooling 48 placebo-controlled RCTs, GLP-1RAs probably neither raise nor lower obesity-related cancer risk — the odds ratios for thyroid, pancreatic, breast, and kidney cancer all cross 1, and the protective signals seen in observational studies don't hold up under randomized evidence.

The authors

A multicenter, physician-led team; the corresponding and senior author is Cho-Han Chiang (Department of Medicine, Mount Auburn Hospital, Harvard Medical School), with Albert Ko, Yu-Cheng Chang, and Furkan Bahar as co–first authors.

Co-authors are spread across Mount Auburn/Harvard, the University at Buffalo, Albert Einstein/Montefiore, the University of Vermont, and National Taiwan University Hospital and Taipei Tzu Chi, among others — many are Taiwanese-American clinical researchers.

Cross-institution systematic reviews and meta-analyses led by residents/fellows are a familiar output for this kind of group. The review was registered on PROSPERO and published in Annals of Internal Medicine.

The problem

GLP-1RAs and GIP/GLP-1 dual agonists are exploding in use, and the most unsettled safety question is cancer.

The worry comes from both directions: on one side, rodent studies show C-cell hyperplasia and medullary thyroid carcinoma with GLP-1RAs, and insurance-claims data flagged higher thyroid cancer in the first year of use; on the other, several population studies report lower incidence of multiple obesity-related cancers among users, read as a weight-loss cancer dividend.

Both directions rest mostly on observational data, conflict with each other, and can be muddied by study design, exposure duration, baseline cancer risk, and confounders like obesity and smoking. What was missing was a synthesis that isolates the randomized evidence and grades certainty cancer by cancer.

The approach

A systematic review and meta-analysis, PRISMA-reported and PROSPERO-registered.

They searched PubMed, Embase, Web of Science, Scopus, and Cochrane through August 2025, including only placebo-controlled RCTs of FDA-approved agents (semaglutide, dulaglutide, exenatide, liraglutide, lixisenatide, tirzepatide) that reported at least one cancer outcome (thyroid, pancreatic, colorectal, gastric, esophageal, liver, gallbladder, breast, ovarian, endometrial, kidney; multiple myeloma; meningioma).

Two reviewers independently screened and extracted, with a third adjudicating.

Risk of bias used Cochrane RoB 2, certainty used GRADE (four levels), publication bias used funnel plots plus Egger's test. Pooling used random effects (Hartung–Knapp–Sidik–Jonkman) with odds ratios (a close approximation to relative risk for rare cancers) and I² for heterogeneity. Because many trials had zero events in a group, they applied a treatment-arm continuity correction to reduce zero-event bias.

Preplanned subgroups covered follow-up and treatment duration, population (T2DM vs. obesity), weight-loss potential, dose, duration of action, drug class, and medullary vs. papillary thyroid strata, plus sensitivity analyses (dropping moderate/high-RoB trials, restricting to semaglutide/tirzepatide, and >156-week follow-up).

Key results

48 trials, 94,245 participants (51,521 on drug, 42,724 on placebo), median follow-up only 70 weeks, 43 rated low risk of bias. Every primary outcome points to "little or no effect": thyroid cancer 0.14% vs 0.07%, OR 1.37 (95% CI 0.82–2.31); pancreatic OR 0.84 (0.53–1.35); breast 0.95 (0.60–1.49); kidney 1.12 (0.78–1.60) — these four at moderate certainty.

Colorectal, esophageal, liver, gallbladder, ovarian, endometrial, multiple myeloma, and meningioma are low-certainty no-effect; gastric is "very uncertain." Thyroid stratified by histology showed no medullary-vs-papillary group difference (P=0.66), with low heterogeneity throughout.

Absolute risk differences work out to a handful of cases more or fewer per 10,000 treated — clinically near zero. One counter-signal is flagged: colorectal cancer was numerically higher, with a trend toward increased risk in trials beyond 156 weeks.

How solid is the evidence

The strength is the design: randomized trials only, which keeps observational confounding and healthy-user bias out, plus per-cancer RoB2 + GRADE grading and consistent subgroup/sensitivity results — the most credible tier in this literature. The softness, which the authors spell out, is twofold.

First, these trials were never designed for cancer; it was an exploratory safety endpoint with adjudication varying across trials, few events, and follow-up typically 1–3 years — far shorter than solid-tumor latency, so "no effect" really reads as "no signal detectable over this short a window," insensitive to both harm and benefit.

Second, without patient-level data they can't verify that randomization actually balanced baseline cancer risk (family history, smoking, prior neoplasms).

They put it honestly: the study supports the absence of an increased cancer risk but was neither designed nor powered to confirm a protective effect. So it falsifies the observational optimism without proving GLP-1RAs prevent cancer.
