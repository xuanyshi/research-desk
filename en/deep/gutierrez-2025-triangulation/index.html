Evidence triangulation in health research

Sirena Gutierrez, M. Maria Glymour, George Davey Smith

European Journal of EpidemiologyCAS 2JCR Q1IF 6.6

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Causal inferenceTriangulationMendelian randomizationInstrumental variables

30-second read

The unit of triangulation is a set of estimates addressing the same causal question. Convergence is interpretable only after aligning estimands, exposures, outcomes, and populations and making major bias sources and directions explicit.

Question
How can studies with different biases support causal inference when no single RCT can answer a long-term health question?
Design
A methodological review organized around alcohol use and dementia, covering confounder-control studies, instrumental variables, negative controls, cross-context comparisons, quantitative bias analysis, and DAG-based assumption mapping.
Evidence strength Moderate
What was assessed
Identification assumptions, comparability conditions, expected bias directions, and cohort, MR, natural-experiment, and negative-control evidence on alcohol and dementia.
Downgrade triggers
This is a framework review. Many decisions require subject-matter knowledge, quantitative triangulation remains under development, and the worked example does not yield a single pooled causal estimate.
Impact on interpretation
The framework can organize evidence and diagnose disagreement, but counts of designs or concordant signs cannot be converted directly into a causal probability.

Triangulation workflow

Full deep read

No single study settles a question. Stack designs whose weaknesses differ, different assumptions, biases that shouldn't line up, and when they still agree, the conclusion holds. That's evidence triangulation.

The authors

Lead author Sirena Gutierrez is a UC San Francisco epidemiologist from M. Maria Glymour's causal-inference and social-epidemiology group (Glymour now chairs epidemiology at Boston University). Senior author George Davey Smith (University of Bristol) pioneered Mendelian randomization and champions evidence triangulation.

The problem

The perfect study doesn't exist. You can't run one that is large, diverse, randomizes every relevant version of a treatment, and tracks every outcome. The authors open with alcohol and dementia.

Plenty of observational work reports that "moderate drinking" comes with better cognition and less dementia, and the press runs with it every year. But those associations are tangled up with socioeconomic status, regional and religious norms, and past addiction, plus reverse causation (people sliding toward dementia drink less), selective survival, and measurement error. Settle it with one big RCT?

The NIH- and industry-funded MACH trial collapsed midway, and even finished it would have answered one narrow slice. The rest has to come from reading many flawed studies together.

The approach

The core move is to make the biases miss each other: find the worst weakness of each design, then bring in evidence that doesn't share it. The new evidence has its own flaws, but if it rests on different assumptions and its biases don't point the same way, and the two still agree, confidence climbs a level.

The tooling is to sort bias into three buckets (confounding, measurement, selection) and use directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) to lay out the untestable assumptions and pick complementary designs whose identifying assumptions differ.

The authors offer a six-piece toolkit. An RCT is the gold standard but often impossible and answers a narrow slice. Instrumental variables and natural experiments use exogenous, near-arbitrarily-timed variation like a policy change to get around unmeasured confounding, on the same assumption set as an RCT.

Mendelian randomization uses genetic variants, valuable because its assumptions differ completely from conventional confounder control. Negative controls find an exposure with a similar confounding structure but no plausible effect, like using paternal smoking to test the intrauterine mechanism behind maternal smoking and offspring obesity.

Cross-context comparison checks the same association across populations with different confounding.

Quantitative bias analysis simply estimates how large the bias is.

Key results

This is a review, so it offers usable conclusions rather than experimental numbers.

Before combining evidence you align the estimands: for LDL cholesterol to coronary heart disease, an RCT estimates the effect of lowering LDL for five years while MR estimates a genetically set, lifelong trajectory, so the MR effect is much larger, but the RCT runs about 40% of MR, a stable ratio you can rescale.

Exposure and outcome definitions need calibrating too (drinks per day versus week, 4+ versus 5+ for a binge, all-cause versus clinically diagnosed dementia).

Often the heterogeneity teaches you more than a single pooled effect; when the direction of bias is clear, an upward-biased and a downward-biased estimate bracket the truth.

How solid is the evidence

This is a methods review, so what you weigh is how mature the framework itself is. The quantitative-combination methods are still developing, and most of the time you can only compare qualitatively. Cross-context and IV work both need large samples, while existing data skews toward high-income, socially advantaged populations.

Triangulation can be gamed too: reporting only the negative control that fits, telling the story after the fact, publication bias. The authors' answer is running the analysis across many reasonable specifications, plus pre-registering the triangulation plan.
