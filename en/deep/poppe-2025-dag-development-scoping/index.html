How to develop causal directed acyclic graphs for observational health research: a scoping review

Louise Poppe, Johan Steen, Wen Wei Loh, Geert Crombez et al.

Health Psychology Review 19 (2025):45–65

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Causal DAGScoping reviewDAG constructionSelection biasMeasurement errorObservational research

30-second read

Only one resource explains how to search for domain knowledge, and selection or measurement nodes are uncommon. The six steps improve transparency but do not guarantee a true graph.

Question
How should DAGs be developed for observational health research, and how well does existing guidance cover domain knowledge, selection, and measurement error?
Design
A scoping review retaining 11 guidance resources from 902 database records and other sources, then proposing six steps from early graph construction to analysis.
Evidence strength Methods review
What was assessed
Purposes, steps, knowledge sources, selection and measurement treatment, and reporting advice across 11 DAG guidance resources.
Downgrade triggers
Graphs limited to measured covariates can omit selection and observation mechanisms; adding those nodes changes identifiable biases and data-collection requirements.
Impact on interpretation
The six steps provide a minimum standard. Edge direction and missing relations still require full-text evidence, expert review, and structural sensitivity analysis.

Six steps for observational-health DAGs

04

Full deep read

Existing DAG guidance broadly agrees on drawing mechanics but rarely explains where domain knowledge comes from or why an edge exists; this scoping review adds selection and measurement processes to a six-step workflow.

The authors

Louise Poppe, Johan Steen, Wen Wei Loh, Peter W. G. Tennant, and colleagues are affiliated with Ghent University, Maastricht University, Emory University, the University of Leeds, the Alan Turing Institute, and other institutions. The team spans health psychology, clinical medicine, statistics, and causal inference.

The problem

DAGs guide covariate selection, yet they are often drawn shortly before analysis with opaque provenance for nodes and edges. The authors review development guidance, compare treatments of confounding, selection, measurement error, and domain knowledge, and propose a minimum practical workflow.

The approach

The scoping review identified 902 unique database records and also considered web and book resources, retaining 11 guidance sources. Two reviewers screened records. Development purpose, steps, knowledge sources, and reporting recommendations were charted. A running example examines physical-activity guidelines and working memory in older adults.

The six steps are: create the DAG early; define exposure and outcome precisely; add common causes; consider selection nodes; consider measurement-error nodes; and use the DAG in analysis. The object for common-cause assessment is an open noncausal exposure–outcome path. A common cause triggers adjustment-set inclusion; selection and measurement nodes reveal biases that ordinary covariate adjustment may not repair.

Key results

Most sources use DAGs to guide analysis, but disagree between sequentially adding confounders and removing edges from a saturated graph. Few discuss selection nodes, only one directly covers measurement-error nodes, and only one source explains how to search the literature for domain knowledge.

The authors therefore make selection and measurement explicit steps and recommend documenting knowledge acquisition and graph decisions. Their example shows how volunteering creates a collider and why questionnaire-measured activity and test-measured working memory should be separated from their underlying constructs.

How solid is the evidence

This is a scoping review of guidance, designed to describe and synthesize recommendations. It does not test which workflow recovers a graph closer to causal truth. Only 11 resources were included, grey literature cannot be exhaustive, and the six steps reflect synthesis rather than prospective validation.

Graph validity still depends on domain knowledge. Missing a common-cause edge can leave residual confounding; adding an unnecessary edge often enlarges the adjustment set; conditioning on a collider or mediator can create bias. Standard DAGs also do not solve treatment-confounder feedback, where ordinary regression may fail.
