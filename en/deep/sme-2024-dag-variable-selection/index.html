Synthesizing Subject-matter Expertise for Variable Selection in Causal Effect Estimation: A Case Study

Julia Debertin, Javier A. Jurado Vélez, Laura Corlin, Bertha Hidalgo et al.

EpidemiologyCAS 2JCR Q1IF 4.4

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Causal inferenceDAG constructionCovariate selectionConfounder adjustmentBias amplification

30-second read

With time-varying covariates, the outcome-driven DAG is closest to the true null. The maximal DAG adds more exposure predictors and leaves greater bias, consistent with bias amplification by near-instruments.

Question
Which DAG-building strategy selects the most effective confounding adjustment set in real data?
Design
A methods case study using the accepted null effect of placebo adherence on five-year mortality in the Coronary Drug Project to compare outcome-driven, trimmed, and maximal DAGs.
Evidence strength Moderate
What was assessed
2,410 placebo-arm participants, three baseline and longitudinal DAG strategies, g-formula and inverse-probability-weighted risk differences, and 500 bootstrap samples.
Downgrade triggers
Evidence comes from one 1970s male myocardial-infarction cohort. Adherence is coarsely measured, candidate confounders are missing, and confidence intervals overlap substantially.
Impact on interpretation
The case supports starting from outcome predictors and treating exposure-only predictors cautiously, but does not establish a universally best DAG-building strategy.

Real-data evaluation of DAG strategies

Full deep read

Using a known-null effect in real data as a gold standard, the DAG built by first listing outcome prognostic factors controlled confounding best, while forcing in exposure predictors amplified bias.

The authors

Senior author Eleanor J. Murray, an epidemiologist at Boston University, runs the Murray Causal Lab and is known for causal-inference methods and science communication as co-host of the Casual Inference podcast. First author Julia Debertin led this DAG covariate-selection case study.

The problem

Standard advice for choosing covariates in causal inference is to draw a DAG and read off the minimal adjustment set that blocks every confounding path.

The gap is that drawing the DAG itself has almost no actionable, evidence-based guidance: most tutorials cover graph theory or the rules for identifying valid adjustment sets, not which variables to put on the graph or how many generations of ancestors to keep adding. Many epidemiologists report this as a real barrier that keeps them from using DAGs at all.

Several building strategies exist (the disjunctive cause criterion, the "kitchen sink" of all exposure predictors, listing all outcome prognostic factors, evidence synthesis for DAGs), but most have only been checked in simulations, and few studies have compared their confounding control on real data.

The core obstacle is that the "true" DAG in real data is never known, so there is no gold standard to score against.

The approach

The authors exploit a special case: the placebo arm of the Coronary Drug Project trial, where the true causal effect of placebo adherence on 5-year mortality is strongly believed to be null, yet the relationship is heavily confounded by baseline and time-varying covariates (the classic "healthy adherer" bias).

Because the truth is known, it becomes a scoring rule: an adjustment set from a DAG closer to the true structure should yield an estimate closer to null. Starting from the DAG implied by the original 1980 analysis, they sequentially built three variants (baseline and longitudinal versions each, six graphs total):

  • outcome-driven DAG: the 40 mortality prognostic factors the trial experts chose without considering adherence.
  • trimmed DAG: a PubMed scoping review removed variables with no evidence of affecting adherence (most lab and ECG measures), then dropped nodes caused by only one other variable.
  • maximal DAG: following the disjunctive cause criterion, added literature- and expert-suggested adherence/mortality causes (hypertension, depression, alcohol) plus covariate–covariate links.

Analysis used the CDP placebo arm, N=2,410 complete cases. Baseline analysis used the plug-in g-formula; the time-varying analysis used inverse probability weighting for time-varying confounding and censoring, with 95% CIs from 500 bootstraps, plus P value functions to compare each result's compatibility with the true null.

Key results

The unadjusted risk difference was 11 percentage points (95% CI 6.6, 16). With baseline covariates only, all three sets performed similarly and all remained biased (incomplete confounding control): outcome-driven 7.0 (2.9, 11), trimmed 7.6 (3.3, 12), maximal 7.6 (3.4, 12).

Adjusting additionally for time-varying covariates separated them: outcome-driven fell to 2.5 (−2.0, 7.0), closest to null; trimmed 3.7 (−1.1, 8.5); maximal only reached 5.2 (0.4, 10), nearly 3 points further from null with a CI excluding zero.

The P value functions gave the same ranking, outcome-driven most compatible with the true null, maximal largely incompatible. Including non-confounding prognostic factors shrank variance without adding bias, whereas the maximal DAG mostly added variables tied to adherence but weakly to mortality ("near-instruments"), triggering bias amplification.

How solid is the evidence

The strength is the design: using a known-null effect as a ruler sidesteps the "true DAG is unknowable" deadlock, and the conclusions line up with theory (instrument-driven bias amplification, harmlessness of prognostic factors), each corroborating the other. External validity is narrow, though.

It is a single case, a 1970s all-male post-MI cohort; the authors concede the DAGs built for the placebo arm may not even hold for the full trial, let alone other questions.

The adherence measure is coarse, clinicians eyeballed the pill bottle into three bins, few ever fell below 20%, and misclassification is likely, which may blunt the time-varying analysis.

Part of why the maximal DAG failed is data-driven: the depression, cognition, and alcohol variables they wanted were mostly unmeasured, so putting them on the graph yielded no adjustment variable, leaving only the bias-amplifying half. So how much of "maximal is worst" is the method versus an accident of this dataset is hard to separate.
