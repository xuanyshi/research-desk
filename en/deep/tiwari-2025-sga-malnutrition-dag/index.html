Directed acyclic graph helps to understand the causality of malnutrition in under-5 children born small for gestational age

Soumya Tiwari, Viswas Chhapola, Nisha Chaudhary, Lokesh Sharma

30-second read

A 21-node DAG yields 12 minimal sufficient sets for future design. Abstract-level evidence and regional restrictions mean the sets hold only if the current graph is correct.

Question
When estimating the total effect of term SGA on under-five malnutrition, what should be adjusted and which mediators or colliders should be avoided?
Design
A DAG-construction study screening about 4,800 South Asian child-nutrition records, extracting 81 factors from 342 abstracts, and drawing edges using temporality, theory, and counterfactual criteria.
Evidence strength Low to moderate
What was assessed
Malnutrition factors from 342 abstracts, a 21-node theoretical DAG, and 12 minimal sufficient adjustment sets generated by DAGitty.
Downgrade triggers
A set is sufficient if it blocks every backdoor path without post-exposure variables; omitted causes or wrong directions could make all 12 sets retain or induce bias.
Impact on interpretation
The graph can guide measurement and primary adjustment. It does not estimate effect magnitude or confirm causality between SGA and malnutrition.

The authors

Soumya Tiwari, Viswas Chhapola, Nisha Chaudhary, and Lokesh Sharma are pediatricians at Lady Hardinge Medical College in New Delhi. The clinical team applies ESC-DAGs-style causal criteria to a South Asian child-nutrition question.

The problem

Term small-for-gestational-age status is associated with under-five malnutrition, while fetal growth restriction, poverty, feeding, and maternal health are connected. Regression that adjusts for everything available can condition on mediators or colliders. The authors first define effects relevant to different stakeholders, then build a graph and adjustment sets for future research.

The approach

Exposure is term SGA and outcome is WHO-defined malnutrition from six months to five years. Estimands are framed for policymakers, clinicians, and parents. PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science, and ProQuest were searched through 15 December 2023, restricted to the previous ten years and the Indian subcontinent.

The rapid narrative review extracts factors from abstracts. Candidate directions pass through temporality, face validity, recourse to theory, and counterfactual thought experiments before entry in DAGitty. The object of adjustment is every backdoor path from SGA to malnutrition. A set is triggered when it blocks all such paths without post-exposure variables; its practical effect is the minimum allowable covariate combination for regression.

Key results

The search found 4,818 records and retained 342 abstracts; 39% of studies were from India and 27% from Bangladesh. Eighty-one factors were grouped into 17 nodes across socioeconomic, parental, child-related, environmental, and political domains.

The final DAG contains 21 nodes. DAGitty identifies 12 minimal sufficient adjustment sets, one containing nine variables. No original effects are pooled and no cohort data estimate the actual effect of term SGA on malnutrition.

How solid is the evidence

Defining the question before using explicit edge criteria is more transparent than P-value-based selection. Evidence extraction remains abstract-only, preventing checks of definitions, timing, adjustment, and risk of bias. The ten-year and regional restrictions can omit common causes, while grouping 81 factors into 17 nodes hides measurement and temporal differences.

The 12 sets are sufficient only under the assumed graph. Similar estimates across them would increase structural compatibility, not validate the graph. There is no expert consensus process, external audit, or data-based sensitivity analysis.
