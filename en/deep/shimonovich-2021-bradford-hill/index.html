Assessing causality in epidemiology: revisiting Bradford Hill to incorporate developments in causal thinking

Michal Shimonovich, Anna Pearce, Hilary Thomson, Katherine Keyes et al.

European Journal of EpidemiologyCAS 2JCR Q1IF 6.6

Source / DOI ↗ Scholar ↗ Bradford HillCausal DAGTriangulationGRADE

30-second read

Question
How can the nine Bradford Hill viewpoints be reinterpreted through DAGs, sufficient-component-cause models, and GRADE?
Design
A methodological essay mapping each Hill viewpoint to DAGs, SCC models, and GRADE, using alcohol consumption and active tuberculosis as a common example.
Evidence strength Limited
What was assessed
Nine Hill viewpoints, three modern causal-assessment frameworks, one alcohol–tuberculosis example, and the conceptual mappings in Tables 4–5.
Downgrade triggers
The article is an essay and the mapping is explicitly preliminary and qualitative. It uses one simplified example and does not compare real evidence-synthesis decisions or rating agreement.
Impact on interpretation
The analysis can update causal-argument language and review checklists, but the revised viewpoints are not a validated causal decision algorithm.

03

Modernizing the Hill viewpoints

04

Full deep read

Hill's nine causal viewpoints laid one by one against DAGs, sufficient-component-cause (SCC) models and GRADE — seeing which hold up, which get rewritten along triangulation/negative-control lines, and which should retire. The common substrate is the potential-outcomes framework.

The authors

Senior author Srinivasa Vittal Katikireddi is a public health professor at Glasgow's MRC/CSO Social and Public Health Sciences Unit; lead author Michal Shimonovich, at the same unit, studies causal assessment in evidence synthesis. Co-author Katherine Keyes is a Columbia University epidemiologist.

The problem

Bradford Hill's nine 1965 viewpoints are still the default tool for judging causality in epidemiology. Hill called them viewpoints and warned against ticking them off as a checklist, yet half a century of practice has treated them as exactly that. In the same half-century, causal inference grew the potential-outcomes framework plus three approaches that operationalise it: DAGs, SCC models (causal pies), and GRADE.

Whether the old and the new overlap, and whether the new can explain the old, had not been compared systematically. Some had put SCC against DAGs, one had put GRADE against Hill, but nobody had laid DAGs and SCC together against all nine viewpoints, and DAGs are precisely what the field has leaned on most.

The question: looking back at Hill through modern causal frameworks, which viewpoints have solid theoretical grounding, which are just the same thing said differently, and which should retire.

The approach

The method is plain: draw a DAG and an SCC pie for each Hill viewpoint, map each against every GRADE domain, and use one running toy example (alcohol → active-TB, with overcrowding as the confounder) to show what each viewpoint "looks like" under the three frameworks.

The key is that all three build on potential outcomes, and the shared currency is exchangeability: DAGs tell you which variables to condition on to get there; SCC models stress how unknown components make exchangeability harder to judge; GRADE simply ranks evidence by how far a design secures exchangeability (RCTs above NRSs).

The fate of the nine sorts into four groups.

  • Four hold up directly: strength, temporality, plausibility, experiment. Strength via DAG bias analysis, SCC showing how competing causes dilute the RR, GRADE offering an RR 2–5 threshold; temporality explicitly encoded by DAGs, which can also probe reverse causation (TB before/after diagnosis); plausibility made explicit by DAGs (mediation, causal chain) and SCC (interaction); experiment via DAGs explaining why randomisation buys exchangeability (randomisation = instrumental variable), extending to natural experiments and the target trial.
  • Specificity gets rewritten. In epidemiology one cause has many effects and one effect many causes, so specificity almost never holds; the authors swap it for falsification / negative controls (head lice as a placebo outcome), which is what remains useful in it.
  • Consistency gets split — the viewpoint closest to triangulation. Hill said consistent results from different approaches strengthen causation; following GRADE, the authors push back: consistency may just be the same bias pulling everything the same way. So it should be split in two — transportability (does the effect hold under a different population/context; DAGs for confounding structure, SCC for competing causes) and unexplained heterogeneity (unexplained statistical inconsistency, which is what actually undermines causation).
  • Largely retired: dose-response is downgraded (a gradient can be manufactured by confounding, and threshold effects are common); coherence and analogy are judged "utility not clearly supported" — the former is indistinguishable from plausibility, the latter gets conflated by GRADE with directness.

To be clear: the three frameworks the paper explicitly compares are DAG / SCC / GRADE; it does not list "triangulation" as one of them. The triangulation logic is buried inside the rewrite of consistency.

Key results

The output is two tables — a mapping table (Table 4) and a conclusions table (Table 5) — not numbers. The core finding: four of the nine genuinely overlap — strength (including analysis of plausible confounding), temporality, plausibility (DAGs encode mediation, SCC encodes interaction), experiment (including how study design bears on exchangeability).

Consistency should be split into transportability vs unexplained heterogeneity; specificity is too rare, so falsification / negative controls serve better; dose-response is less useful than assumed, since confounding alone can produce a gradient; coherence and analogy are of doubtful utility.

On triangulation they never use the word, but the consistency passage is blunt: pointing to the same conclusion does not mean the results are statistically consistent or poolable, and agreement may just be shared bias — which is exactly the starting point of Lawlor/Davey Smith triangulation, only approached here from the Hill side.

The magnitude demonstration sits in the SCC table (alcohol alone RR 3.0, overcrowding alone 3.5 vs 2.0 across two populations, both together 4.5), showing how competing causes and reference-group prevalence shift the RR.

How solid is the evidence

This is an essay, and the authors keep saying "preliminary" and "tentative." Several limits I accept:

  • A single deliberately simplified alcohol → TB toy example can't carry a general claim that "every viewpoint works this way in real studies"; swap in a messy, poorly-measured, many-confounder problem and the mapping blurs.
  • They compare only the three "best-known" frameworks (DAG / SCC / GRADE), explicitly skipping IARC, teratogenicity criteria and others, and traditions outside potential outcomes don't enter.
  • They themselves waver on whether SCC belongs, conceding it is more a description of causal reality and the least used.
  • The judgments are qualitative: which viewpoint "survives," which is "utility not clearly supported," is largely the authors' reading, not a reproducible test.

To their credit the boundaries are drawn honestly — no overselling this as a "new set of causal criteria," just an opening bid inviting argument.
